marketing, advertising, landing-page copy, and brand voice. This is the *raw material* shelf;
the app-level brand decisions live in `thinking/app/brand-and-voice-notes.md` and naming in

## Usage discipline (read before using anything here)

Marketing copy is outward-facing and hard to retract once published. Every line below is tagged:

- **✅ OURS** — original Croft framing (or a public-domain source); use freely, attribute to Croft.
- **📚 CITE** — a real external idea/study; use as *support/reference*, attribute to the source, do
  **not** present as our slogan.
- **⚠️ CLEARANCE** — copyrighted third-party text (film/TV/song dialogue). Treat as **inspiration
  only**; do **not** reproduce verbatim in commercial material (paid app-store listing, ads, merch)
  without legal review / a license. A *stylistic nod* ("IYKYK") is fine and usually cooler.
- **❓ UNVERIFIED** — attribution or wording is not confirmed; paraphrase, don't quote-and-attribute.

When in doubt, paraphrase into our own voice. Fact-check provenance lives in the per-transcript
`-FACTCHECK.md` files; this file repeats only the usage-relevant verdict.

## 2. The "corporation vs. person" / crowding-out theme (mixed — read flags)

A core Croft theme: **financializing every interaction crowds out the intrinsic motivation that
makes community real.** Strong material, but mind the provenance:

- ❓ **The door-holding anecdote** ("difference between a corporation and a person? have you ever
  held the door open… did you ask for money first? … *that's* the difference"). Widely retold; often
  attributed to **Robert Hare** in the documentary **_The Corporation_ (2003)** — but the **exact
  exchange is NOT verifiably sourced** to the film. **Use as paraphrased "philosophical folklore,"
  do not attribute the verbatim quote.** (FACTCHECK: PARTLY/UNVERIFIABLE.)
- 📚 **The "overjustification effect" / motivation crowding-out** — real, citable: **Gneezy &
  Rustichini, "A Fine is a Price," _Journal of Legal Studies_ (2000)** — Israeli daycares that
  introduced a late-pickup fine saw lateness *increase* (parents reframed a social obligation as a
  purchasable price). Strong evidence line; cite it.
- ✅/📚 **Thurlow / Poynder (1844, public domain):** *"A corporation has no soul to damn, and no body
  to kick."* Safe to quote with attribution.
- 📚 **Dan Ariely** — *social norms vs. market norms* (_Predictably Irrational_, 2008). Cite as the
  framework, not a slogan.
- 📚 **Elinor Ostrom, _Governing the Commons_ (1990; Nobel 2009)** — the commons can be
  community-governed without a market or a state; the intellectual backbone of the cooperative
  thesis. Cite for credibility, not as ad copy.

## 3. Cultural touchstones & tie-in ideas (⚠️ clearance — inspiration, not verbatim)

The pull is to **reach people where they are** (younger, online, micro-community-native) without
the corporate smell. The strongest tie-in raised so far:

- ⚠️ **Euphoria (HBO) — Rue's line, S1:** *"that's the thing about the internet… sometimes 10
  people can feel like the whole world."* It's an exact match for the **digital-living-room /
  small-tight-group** value prop. **But it's HBO-copyrighted dialogue** — do **not** put the verbatim
  quote on a paid listing, ad, or merch without clearance. In-show context is melancholic
  (isolation); **recontextualize toward closeness/loyalty**, and prefer a **stylistic nod** over the
  literal line. Branding angles it inspires (✅ ours, derived):
  - **"The Ten" / cap-at-10 groups** — *"Because sometimes, ten people are the whole world."*
    (our paraphrase — clears the verbatim-quote risk).
  - **"Small circles, big worlds." / "Find your world."** — abstract, clean, no IP exposure.
  - **"That's the thing about the internet…"** — an *evocative fragment*; lower risk than the full
    line, but still a recognizable nod — run it past legal before commercial use.
- **Positioning that fits the demographic (✅ ours):** the **digital living room** vs the public
  shouting-square; *invite-only / tight-knit*; **IYKYK** discovery (don't explain the reference —
  letting people find it makes it feel tapped-in). Tone: low-fi, lowercase, candid, made-by-a-friend
  not by a marketing board. (These pair with the `thinking/app/` ponds/pads + "Croft Group" framing.)

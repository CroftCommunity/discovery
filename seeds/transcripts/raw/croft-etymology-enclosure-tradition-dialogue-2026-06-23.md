# SECTION A — The produced reference document (as pasted)

## "Croft" — Primary-Source Reference

A dated reference for the noun *croft*, the agent noun *crofter*, and the verb/gerund *crofting*,
drawn from open scholarly dictionaries. Two parts: (1) the complete Middle English Dictionary
quotation set, transcribed verbatim with dates; (2) findings on whether the word travelled outward
during migration.

Conventions in the dating notation below follow the MED's own system. A date like `c1300` means
"around 1300." A date like `a1400(1251)` means the surviving manuscript is from before 1400 but the
text it copies dates to 1251. Square brackets around a quotation are the MED's marking for a Latin or
Anglo-Latin record (the surrounding document is in Latin, with *croft* embedded as the vernacular
land-term).

#### What the MED entry shows for the thesis

The note "OE" in the etymology field, with no clean cognate chain, matches what the larger
dictionaries say: the origin is Old English but ultimately uncertain.

The four-way sense split (working plot / figurative / any-enclosure-including-tournament-field-and-hell
/ surname) is the strongest internal evidence that the word names a *concept* (enclosed ground put to
use) rather than a single fixed referent. A word that can mean a field of oats, a tournament arena, and
hell-as-an-enclosure within the same century is a spatial concept, not a definition.

The place-name and surname sections are enormous relative to the prose citations. Most of the dated
record for *croft* is fossilized in toponyms (Stancroft, Oxecroft, Barleycroft) and locative surnames
(Hugo de Croft, 1163). This is exactly the pattern of a word that was general property of the language
and spread by ordinary land-naming, long before any legal codification.

### Part 2: Did the word travel outward during migration?

Short version: the *English place-name element* spread internally and fossilized across Britain
centuries before any emigration. But during the later emigrations (17th to 19th century), the word
"croft" itself largely did **not** transplant as living vocabulary. What travelled was either the
Gaelic word, or the practice under a different name, or the surname.

#### The internal spread came first (and is the real story of ubiquity)

Before any transatlantic migration, *-croft* was already saturating British toponymy through ordinary
Anglo-Saxon and later medieval land-naming. The MED place-name list above is one county-by-county slice
of this. This is the "western ubiquity" point: the word was common property of English (and Lowland
Scots, from c.1250) and spread by people naming their fields, not by any single authority.

#### The diaspora carried Gaelic, not the English word

The Highland Clearances (roughly 1750 to 1860) scattered Highlanders to Nova Scotia, Prince Edward
Island, New Brunswick, Ontario, the Carolinas, and later Australia and New Zealand. Crucially, what
these communities carried was the **Scottish Gaelic** language and its own term, *croitearachd*, not
the English noun "croft." The Gaelic for a croft is *croit*. So in the places where crofters actually
went in large numbers, the word that survived in daily speech was the Gaelic one, and Gaelic place
names (not "-croft" names) are what dot Nova Scotia and parts of New Zealand today.

This is itself evidence for the "outcome, not origin" framing. The English word "croft" was tied
closely enough to its British home that emigrant Gaelic-speakers did not export it; they exported
*croit* and *croitearachd*. The English "croft/crofting" vocabulary stayed bound to Britain and, later,
to the British administrative and legal apparatus.

#### Where the *practice* went, the *word* often changed

The clearest documented case is the Isle of Man. There, "crofter" and "crofting" are applied generally
to small farmers, but the words came into use only recently and were retrofitted onto a way of life that
had existed for centuries under entirely different local-legal names: "Tenant Julayne" in the 15th to
16th centuries, "Cotler" and "Intackholder" in the 17th to 18th. Same practice, different and older
words; "crofting" is a late label.

In North Carolina, the sources speak of "Highland crofters (tenant farmers)" being recruited to the
Sandhills, but this is largely the language of the recruiting schemes and later historians describing
the settlers, rather than evidence that "crofting" became a living American agricultural term. The
settlers retained Gaelic and Presbyterianism; the word "crofting" did not take root as the name of a
North American land system.

#### What did travel reliably: the surname

The one form of the word that genuinely went global is the **surname** Croft (and its compounds). The
locative surname is attested from 1163 (Hugo de Croft) and 1193 (Gilbertus de Croft) in the MED list
above, derived from people living at or by a croft. Surnames travel with people regardless of whether
the underlying common noun stays current, so Croft as a family name spread throughout the Anglophone
diaspora (North America, Australia, New Zealand, South Africa) even where "croft" as an everyday word
for a small field had become archaic or never arrived.

#### The cognate angle (further afield in the West Germanic family, not via export)

The major dictionaries derive or relate the word to Dutch *kroft* / *krocht*, meaning high-and-dry land
or a field on the downs, and to Middle Low German *kroch* for an enclosed piece of farmland or pasture.
This is shared West Germanic inheritance rather than export from English. It is the deeper basis for the
"western ubiquity" intuition: the concept of named enclosed ground sits across the West Germanic
languages, which is why no clean single-source etymology has ever stuck.

#### Bottom line on migration

- The word saturated **British** place names by ordinary internal spread, centuries before emigration.
  That is the ubiquity.
- During emigration, **Gaelic** *croit* / *croitearachd* travelled with Highland settlers, not the
  English "croft."
- The **practice** often arrived under older local names (Isle of Man) or simply as "tenant farming."
- The **surname** Croft travelled everywhere and is the word's most durable global footprint.
- The **cognates** (Dutch *krocht*, Low German *kroch*) reflect shared West Germanic inheritance,
  supporting "the concept is widespread" over "the word has one source."

### Part 3: The commons-rebellion tradition (and why the croft is the exception)

The croft makes sense as the inverse of historical enclosure. Crofting tenure pairs a secure individual
plot (the croft) with a retained share in common grazing held by the township. English parliamentary
enclosure did the opposite: it took the open commons and shared waste and converted them into
consolidated private holdings, extinguishing common right. The same word, "enclosure," sits at the
centre of both stories pointing opposite ways.

The English record below is overwhelmingly a record of resistance in defeat. The commoners rose, and
lost, repeatedly, and the verse that survives is mostly lament. The Highland crofting settlement of 1886
is the rare case where the resistance won something written into statute. That contrast is the whole
reason the croft reads as a "balance struck" rather than just another enclosure.

Entries are chronological, with sources.

#### 1381: John Ball and the Peasants' Revolt

The root couplet of English commoner sentiment, recorded by contemporary chroniclers and traditionally
attributed to the preaching of the priest John Ball:

"When Adam delved and Eve span, / Who was then the gentleman?"

It predates enclosure proper, but it is the bedrock idea the later risings drew on: that hierarchy over
land and labour is made, not natural. Ball was hanged, drawn, and quartered.

Source: chronicle tradition (Thomas Walsingham and others); widely anthologised.

#### 1549: Kett's Rebellion (Norfolk)

The classic Tudor anti-enclosure rising. The telling detail is that Robert Kett was himself a minor
landholder who had enclosed common land, but when rioters threw down his hedges he switched sides and
led them. The rebels camped on Mousehold Heath outside Norwich and held court under a tree they called
the Oak of Reformation, issuing a list of demands against enclosure and landlord abuses. The signature
act of the whole tradition is here: tearing down the hedges. Kett was hanged from the walls of Norwich
Castle.

Source: Tudor Rebellions accounts; Kett's Demands survive as a primary document.

#### 1607: The Midland Revolt and the Newton Rebellion

The rising where the protest vocabulary itself was coined. The Earl of Shrewsbury reported a crowd
"busily digging" in the Newton enclosures who called themselves, in one of the earliest uses of the
term, "levellers." The two great rebel names come straight from the physical acts against enclosure:
they levelled the hedges and dug up the ditches, so were called "Levellers or Diggers."

The leader was a tinker from Desborough, John Reynolds, nicknamed "Captain Pouch." He claimed authority
from both the King and the Lord of Heaven to destroy enclosures, and promised to protect the protesters
with the contents of the pouch at his side, urging them to use no violence. The trigger was the Tresham
family enclosing the Brand, common land in Rockingham Forest long used for grazing, especially the pigs
that were a winter lifeline.

The crowds were large (around 3,000 at Hillmorton in Warwickshire and 5,000 at Cotesbach in
Leicestershire; a curfew in Leicester; a warning gibbet pulled down). It ended at Newton on 8 June 1607,
where over a thousand protesters including women and children pulled down hedges and filled ditches.

Two ironies define it. First, the deputy lieutenant ordered to suppress them, Edward Montagu, had
himself spoken against enclosure in Parliament. Second, the local militia refused the call-up, so the
landowners used their own servants. The royal proclamation was read twice, the rebels held, the gentry
charged. Forty to fifty were killed; ringleaders were hanged and quartered and their bodies displayed
across Northamptonshire. When Captain Pouch's protective pouch was opened, it held, per the chronicle,
"onely a peece of greene cheese."

Aftermath: the Tresham family declined; the Montagu family rose to become Dukes of Buccleuch, among the
largest landowners in Britain. Newton was effectively the last open armed conflict between English
peasantry and gentry. The revolt is also widely thought to lie behind Shakespeare's Coriolanus (written
about 1607-8), which opens on a grain riot driven by hoarding.

Sources: Wikipedia, "Midland Revolt"; Save Weekley Hall Wood, "Newton Rebellion"; Northamptonshire
Battlefields Society; The History of England podcast (ep. 331); MoneyWeek (8 June 1607).

#### 1649: The Diggers (True Levellers)

The high point of the idea, and the most quotable primary source. On 1 April 1649 Gerrard Winstanley and
a small band began cultivating the common at St George's Hill in Surrey, calling themselves the True
Levellers. Their manifesto, The True Levellers Standard Advanced (26 April 1649), declared the aim to
make "the Earth a common treasury for all, both rich and poor." Winstanley argued that England would not
be a free people until the landless poor had a free allowance to dig and labour the commons, and he
traced landlordism to the "Norman power," treating the 1066 Conquest as the original theft. That "Norman
Yoke" idea runs through the whole 17th-century radical tradition. The colony was broken up by hired
thugs and lawsuits within a year.

Sources: The True Levellers Standard Advanced (1649), primary text; Wikipedia, "Gerrard Winstanley";
Spartacus Educational; pasttense.co.uk.

#### Mid-to-late 18th century: "The Goose and the Common"

Anonymous protest verse, and the one whose provenance is most often stated wrongly. The common claim
that it is "17th century" is not supported. Composition is usually placed in the 1750s or 1760s as
enclosure legislation accelerated, with a frequently cited date of 1764. The earliest hard attestations
are later: an 1816 reference in The Gentleman's Mathematical Companion, noting it seen on a handbill at
Plaistow protesting a Bill to enclose Epping Forest, and the first recorded four-stanza form in 1821 in
The Tickler Magazine. Treat any pre-1800 text as reconstructed.

The most-quoted couplet, in one common form:

"The law locks up the man or woman / Who steals the goose from off the common, / But lets the greater
felon loose / Who steals the common from the goose."

Sources: Wikipedia, "The Goose and the Common"; Cato Street Conspiracy resources; David A. Harley
(substack); unionsong.com.

#### Early 19th century: John Clare

The interior, emotional record of enclosure, written by an actual agricultural labourer from Helpston in
Northamptonshire who lived through the enclosure of his own parish (award 1820).

"The Mores" (sometimes "The Moors"): heroic couplets, structured as a before-and-after. Clare conjures
the open, unfenced moorland of his childhood as a boundless common, then stages enclosure as conquest
and imprisonment, with fences and trespass signs replacing freedom of movement and fencing out
commoners, birds, and brooks alike. Enclosure is named as theft of a shared world.

"Remembrances": more elegiac and personal. A poem of loss and naming, listing specific beloved places
from his boyhood by their local names and mourning each as gone, with enclosure the destroyer in the
background. The power is the specificity: he grieves particular trees and nooks the way one grieves
individuals.

Full texts are public domain (Clare died 1864) and available via the Poetry Foundation and standard
Clare editions. Not reproduced here. [Later companion: the full public-domain texts of *The Mores*,
*Remembrances*, and *To a Fallen Elm* were supplied 2026-06-23 and filed at
`croft-clare-enclosure-poems-2026-06-23.md`.]

#### 1867: The theoretical capstone

Karl Marx, in Capital vol. 1, gave the process its lasting name, "so-called primitive accumulation,"
describing enclosure as the forcible expropriation of the people from the soil, what he called "the
systematic theft of communal property."

Source: Marx, Capital vol. 1 (1867); phrase as cited in Science for the People, "The Goose on the
Common."

#### The Scottish contrast

Nearly every English example above ends in an execution or a dispersal. The croft is the exception. The
Gaelic world carried the underlying sentiment in the idea of dùthchas, the inherited collective right to
live on the land, the unity of people and place. And the Highland crofting story is the rare case where
the resistance won a settlement rather than a scaffold.

The Crofters' War of the 1880s, the rent strikes and land-raids onto the common grazings at the Braes
(1882) and Glendale (1883), produced not hangings but the Napier Commission (report 1884) and then the
Crofters Holdings (Scotland) Act 1886. The Commission preserved, in nearly 3,400 pages, the verbatim
testimony of 704 witnesses, most of them crofters speaking in Gaelic. The first witness, Angus Stewart
of Braes on Skye (8 May 1883), asked for assurance against eviction, got none, and summarised the remedy
as "give us land." The Act gave security of tenure, heritability, and fair rents, and protected the
common grazings, though it did not restore the land already cleared.

Sources: see Part 2 plus Napier Commission materials (Scotland's People; High Life Highland; History
Workshop; Angus Macleod Archive); Historic Environment Scotland blog.

### Part 4: The living afterlife (Napier's voices and the right to roam)

Part 3 closed on the Scottish settlement in summary. This part does two things. First it records the
Napier testimony as a primary archive worth reading directly. Then it follows the same commons argument
into the present through the right-to-roam movement, where it loops back, by coincidence of family and
place, to the 1607 Newton rising.

#### The Napier transcripts as a primary archive

The Royal Commission of 1883-84 left the largest body of ordinary commoners' speech in the whole
tradition. The evidence is verbatim question-and-answer, much of it given in Gaelic through an
interpreter, with each township electing a delegate to carry its grievances. On Skye, 72 percent of
those who appeared (124 of 172) were crofters or cottars, so this is largely the people's own voice
rather than their representatives'.

Where to read it:

- West Highland College UHI at Mallaig digitised the thousands of pages, hosted on the UHI Centre for
  History website.
- Cleaned-up transcripts are posted location by location at napier-skye.blogspot.com.

Four testimonies worth reading in full:

Angus Stewart, the first witness (Braes, Skye, 8 May 1883). Before giving substantive evidence he asked
for a guarantee against eviction, since he could not describe his people's suffering without naming the
landlord who caused it. He had already been served with removal: "I would not have a fire in my house at
Whitsunday." Napier admitted on the record that the Commission could not protect him.

Donald Nicolson, aged 78 (Uig, Skye, 10 May 1883). Asked if he was a crofter, he said he had been, but
the croft was taken and he was now going from house to house. His rent had been doubled and he was
evicted over one extra pound. The tacksman warned every other crofter that anyone who sheltered him
would face the same, so "not one of them would let me into his house, they were so afraid." He was put
out of a shed, then a stable, then served an interdict against entering the land at all.

The Solitote petition (Uig, Skye), read by delegate John Macdonald. A holding once worked by one crofter
was now split among eighteen families, the rent risen from about £9 to over £22. They were not allowed
to keep a cow or a sheep, were forbidden to cut heather to bind their roofs or rushes to thatch them,
and were barred from cutting seaweed to manure the ground. Their summary: "we really have nothing now."
One delegate added that with more land they would not have to risk their lives at sea in rough weather.

Tiree (Balemartine delegate). The case of a crofter evicted for no good reason, forbidden to build a
house anywhere on the island, with any crofter offering even one night's shelter threatened with instant
eviction.

On the Battle of the Braes itself (April 1882), the Sheriff of Inverness-shire, William Ivory, reported
that the crowd, many women and children among them, "attacked us furiously first with curses, and then
with sticks, stones and other missiles" (NRS, GD1/36/1/3/52).

Underneath the grievances sat dùthchas, the Gaelic sense that people, land, and culture are bound
together by a right to live and work the land from time immemorial. The clearances were felt as a breach
of that compact, not merely a rent dispute.

The settlement and its limits: the 1884 Franchise Act gave crofters the vote (Ross-shire's electorate
rose from 1,720 to 10,265), four pro-crofter MPs were returned in 1885, and the 1886 Act followed. John
Lorne Campbell later called it "the Magna Carta of the Highlands and Islands." But the Commission was
landlord-heavy, recommended assisted emigration for the poorest tenants below a £6 rent, and the Act did
not restore the cleared land, so land raids on Lewis continued for decades.

#### Nick Hayes and the Right to Roam

Who he is: Nick Hayes is a British writer, illustrator, and printmaker, known for a bold black-and-white
woodcut style. He is an artist-activist rather than an academic historian, and he co-founded the Right to
Roam campaign with Guy Shrubsole, author of Who Owns England?

The book: The Book of Trespass: Crossing the Lines That Divide Us, published by Bloomsbury in August
2020, a bestseller praised by Robert Macfarlane and George Monbiot. It is a hybrid of nature writing,
enclosure history, and political argument, illustrated with his own prints. Its structure is the device:
in each chapter Hayes physically trespasses onto a great private estate and uses the crossing as a
doorway into a piece of the history of enclosure. He followed it in 2022 with The Trespasser's Companion,
a practical manifesto for the access movement.

His argument is the same inversion running through this whole file, from Winstanley to the goose poem:
the enclosure is the crime, not the walking across it. His aphorism is "the fence creates the crime." He
opens with William the Conqueror and Norman forest law, peasants ousted and villages burned to make a
royal hunting ground, the same Norman Yoke the 17th-century radicals invoked.

His headline figures: the public is shut out of about 92 percent of the land in England and about 97
percent of the rivers; public footpaths have roughly halved to around 118,000 miles since the 19th
century; hereditary aristocrats still hold about a third of Britain.

#### The 1607-to-2020 loop

One of the estates Hayes trespasses into is Boughton House in Northamptonshire, an 11,000-acre seat of
the Duke of Buccleuch, who at roughly a quarter-million acres is the largest private landowner in
Britain. The Buccleuch dukedom is the Montagu line. That is the same Montagu family whose ancestor, Sir
Edward Montagu, was sent to suppress Captain Pouch and the Newton rebels in 1607, and who rose afterward
into one of the great landholdings of the country. Hayes climbing that wall in 2020 closes a four-hundred-year
loop in the same Northamptonshire soil.

The loop is still live. Hayes notes Boughton selling firewood back to local people at around £95 a cubic
metre, wood it was once a common right to gather, and a fight over Weekley Hall Wood, where the estate
planned to fell about 10 acres used by some 7,000 people a week. Weekley Hall Wood sits on that same
Buccleuch estate, on the very ground of the 1607 rising.

#### Synthesis

Scotland's commoners won twice. They froze the balance into statute with the croft and common grazings
in 1886, and won the right to walk the land itself with the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003. England's
commoners lost the commons and got neither back. That is precisely why Hayes has to trespass to make his
point: he is asserting, as a crime, what a Scot now holds by law. The croft and the Scottish right to
roam are the same victory the English tradition never managed.

### Part 5: Enclosure as a global pattern

The privatising of common land is not an English peculiarity. Historians treat it as a recurring move
that appears wherever a society shifts toward intensive agriculture, markets, or centralised state
power. England is the best-documented case, not the only one.

Some scholars argue for a single long-term global movement to privatise the commons that radiated
outward from the British Isles, with the peak periods of English enclosure (Tudor and late 18th century)
lining up with bursts of imperial expansion (E. P. Thompson; Allan Greer, American Historical Review,
2012). The export was literal as well as legal. As Peter Linebaugh and Marcus Rediker put it, the
English took overseas possession "by building fences and hedges," the markers of enclosure and private
property. Marx named the underlying engine "primitive accumulation"; David Harvey updated it to
"accumulation by dispossession" to stress that it is ongoing, not a one-time event.

A note on the deep layer: the first shift from foraging commons to owned, bounded fields happened with
the Neolithic transition to settled agriculture and left no documents, only field boundaries and grain
stores in the archaeology. That layer is inferred, not recorded. The cases below are historically
attested.

#### Rome: the ager publicus and the Gracchi (133 BC)

The ager publicus was Roman state land, usually taken by conquest. Wealthy elites steadily
over-occupied it beyond the legal 500-iugera limit, displacing smallholders into large slave-worked
estates (latifundia). In 133 BC the tribune Tiberius Sempronius Gracchus passed the Lex Sempronia
Agraria to reimpose the limit and redistribute the excess to poor citizens through a three-man
commission. Boundary stones (cippi) show the commissioners distributed roughly 3,268 square kilometres
to about 15,000 households between 133 and 129 BC. Tiberius was killed in 133, his brother Gaius in 121,
and the reforms were largely reversed, with land re-concentrating in elite hands.

Caveat for accuracy: some modern scholars note that the ancient "latifundia on public land" narrative is
only partly supported by archaeology, and that much redistribution came at the expense of Italian allies
who were evicted from ancestral land, which helped trigger the Social War (91 to 87 BC).

Sources: Wikipedia ("Ager publicus," "Lex Sempronia," "Gracchi brothers," "Tiberius Gracchus");
Britannica ("Agrarian law").

#### England: the documented core

Covered in Parts 3 and 4. The first Inclosure-relevant statutes reach back to the 13th century (Statute
of Merton, 1235), the Tudor and Georgian peaks did the main work, and the tradition of resistance runs
from Kett to the Diggers to John Clare. This is the case with the richest paper trail, which is why it
anchors the rest.

#### The colonial template: India and Africa

Enclosure became a tool of conquest. In Bengal, the Permanent Settlement of 1793 imposed private
property on Indian land, making zamindars into landlords with power to set revenue terms and dispossess
any peasant who could not pay. Land tax reached about 60 percent of government revenue by 1841. The legal
machinery hardened with the Land Acquisition Act of 1894, whose elastic notion of "public purpose"
allowed expropriation even for private companies, a framework the postcolonial state inherited wholesale.

In Africa, colonial states used corvée labour and coercive taxes to force rural people into labouring
for the state or European enterprise. Scholars describe Africa today being re-divided in a repeat of the
imperial land grab, this time with China and the United States in the role the Europeans once held.

Sources: Allan Greer, AHR (2012); Overstory Alliance (Medium); Journal of Peasant Studies (China and
India land dispossession); International Socialist Review (Harvey, accumulation by dispossession).

#### Russia: the commune and Stolypin (1906 to 1914)

Russian peasants held land through the commune (obshchina or mir), which in much of European Russia
practised open-field farming with periodic repartition of strips to even out household need. After the
1861 emancipation, land was vested in the commune, not individual households. Reformers blamed communal
tenure for backwardness.

Prime Minister Pyotr Stolypin set out to break it. His decree of 9 November 1906 gave each household the
right to claim individual ownership and leave the commune, backed by the Peasant Land Bank and
consolidation into separate farmsteads (khutors and otrubs). One study describes the aim as diligent
farmers leaving the commune to set up "their own physically enclosed forms." Stolypin called it a "wager
on the strong and sober," a deliberate bid to create conservative smallholders as a bulwark against the
unrest of the 1905 Revolution. By 1915 about 26 percent of commune members (some 2.5 million
householders) had taken up the option, acquiring around 15 percent of communal holdings. The program was
cut short by war and revolution, and the Bolsheviks first reversed it, then later collectivised, the
opposite extreme.

Sources: Wikipedia ("Stolypin reform," "Peasant commune"); Britannica ("Stolypin land reform"); Judith
Pallot, Land Reform in Russia 1906-1917 (Oxford, 1999); Encyclopedia of Ukraine.

#### Mexico: the ejido pendulum

Mexico is the clearest back-and-forth. Communities held land communally before and through the colonial
period. The ejido was communal land, legally inalienable and imprescriptible, administered through the
village Assembly rather than titled to individuals, and it included crop plots, common woodlots, and
pasture.

The 19th-century Liberal Reform tried to dissolve this. The Ley Lerdo of 25 June 1856 forced civil and
ecclesiastical corporations, including indigenous communities, to subdivide and sell communal land
(disentailment), and dispossession of village lands accelerated under the Porfiriato. Land hunger was a
central driver of the Mexican Revolution of 1910 (Zapata's "Tierra y Libertad"), and the 1917
Constitution's Article 27 reconstituted the ejido. Over the following 85 years the post-revolutionary
state redistributed more than half of Mexico's arable land in ejido form.

Then the pendulum swung back. In 1992 (by an amendment passed in December 1991) President Carlos Salinas
de Gortari reformed Article 27 to permit the sale and privatisation of ejido land, tied to the NAFTA
negotiations. Most scholars treat this as the death blow to collective tenure. It was a direct trigger
for the Zapatista uprising that began on 1 January 1994, the day NAFTA took effect.

Sources: Wikipedia ("Ejido," "Land reform in Mexico"); Grokipedia ("Land reform in Mexico"); Spectre
Journal; Notre Dame Law (Article 27 and Mexican Land Reform); legalculturessubsoil.ilcs.sas.ac.uk.

#### The American West: privatising the public domain

The clearest large-scale enclosure after England. The US land system was designed around the assumption
that the vast territory taken through conquest and settler colonialism would be privatised, on exactly
the logic used in Britain, that private ownership would maximise effort and development.

Three layers:

The Homestead Act of 1862 distributed the federal public domain to settlers. One account calls it
perhaps the greatest privatisation act in American history, eventually transferring about 270 million
acres, nearly 10 percent of the United States, to some 1.6 million homesteaders.

Barbed wire was the literal enclosure. Joseph Glidden's 1874 patent, nicknamed "the Winner," made it
cheap to fence the open range. Through the 1880s, large cattle outfits fenced vast tracts including
public land and water, setting off the fence-cutting wars, fiercest in Texas, where cutters (calling
themselves names like the Owls and Blue Devils) snipped wire at night to reopen the commons. By 1884
fence-cutting had caused millions in damage and Governor Ireland called a special legislative session
that made it a felony. In New Mexico, smaller Mexicano farmers cut the fences of Anglo land companies
enclosing the common land of old Spanish grants. A standard reference notes plainly that these range
wars were "similar to the disputes which resulted from enclosure laws in England."

The Dawes Act of 1887 (General Allotment Act) was enclosure aimed at the Native commons, the closest
analogue to the Highland clearances. It subdivided tribal communal landholding into individual 160-acre
allotments, with Senator Henry Dawes framing the goal as ridding the nation of "tribalism through the
virtues of private property." Once each family had a parcel, the declared surplus was sold to settlers,
speculators, and railroads. Native-held land fell from about 138 million acres in 1887 to 48 million by
the time allotment ended. One survey notes the barbed wire itself "kept more land away from Indian
tribes, who had never envisioned a culture that would claim to own land," which is the point about
ownership being a recent technical overlay, stated from the other side.

Sources: National Archives ("Homestead Act," "Barbed Wire," Glidden patent); Wikipedia ("Homestead
Acts," "Dawes Act," "Fence Cutting Wars," "Open range," "Barbed wire"); Mackinac Center; Lumen Learning
(US History II); AAG Review of Books (public lands comparison); University of Richmond, "Mapping the
Homestead Act."

#### Synthesis

The recurring justification is the giveaway. In every case communal tenure is cast as backward and
inefficient, and private parceling as improvement and civilization. It is the same "Improvement"
rhetoric that drove the Highland Clearances, now spoken in Bengal, on the Russian steppe, in the Mexican
village, on the Great Plains, and on the Sioux reservation. The actors and centuries change. The move
does not: a shared space is declared a problem, surveyed into lots, fenced, and assigned to individuals,
with the surplus people pushed to the margins or off the land.

This is why the croft and the Scottish right to roam look less like a local curiosity and more like one
of the few places the near-universal enclosure move was halted and partly reversed. Mexico's pendulum
shows the commons can also return, then be taken again. Ownership of land is a recent technical notion
laid over ground that predates it and will outlast it, which is exactly why the commons keeps reappearing
as a demand even after each enclosure.

The croft is an enclosure in the linguistic sense (a bounded individual plot). But the crofting

preserved against an outside enemy. The sequence is layered. First there was runrig (the older
communal township, the *baile*) — shared, periodically reallocated arable strips plus common grazing.

enclosure in all but name). Crofting communities were a *product* of that clearance — people pushed
onto small fixed coastal plots — not a survival from before it. The croft was a step toward
individualization relative to runrig. But it kept the common grazing, and that retained common became
the thing the 1886 settlement crystallized. So: communal runrig → enclosure-logic of clearance → the
croft as a negotiated re-balancing (private plot + surviving common). The croft is the settlement

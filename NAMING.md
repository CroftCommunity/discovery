# Naming: "Croft" is the center of gravity

date: 2026-06-15

## The decision

**Croft** is the project's name center of gravity. It already aligns with the existing
structure: the GitHub org is `CroftCommunity`, the working tree is `CroftC`. The earlier
candidate names (Sovereign Commons, Alt.Drive, Altis, Loci, Lantern, etc. — dossier §1.1, §9)
are now subordinate to / superseded by Croft as the anchor. Sub-product names can still live
under it, but Croft is the through-line.

## Why "Croft" — and why it earns its power by being unromantic

The crofting research deliberately stripped the romance, and that is exactly what makes the
name strong. The weak analogy ("crofters fought landlords, we fight platforms, eternal
struggle") is the tartan-tinged version. The strong version — the one the history actually
supports — is sharper because it is less heroic:

- **The croft was engineered dependency dressed as tenure.** Holdings were deliberately
  sized too small to live on, so the crofter had to sell labour to the same party that
  controlled the land (kelp, fishing). That is the precise shape of a user's relationship
  with a platform: given just enough space to be present, never enough to be independent,
  the deficit filled by working for the landlord (attention, content, data as the kelp).
  Naming the project after the croft names the **trap** — a permanent reminder of the default
  arrangement we exist to invert.

- **The win was real but partial, and named grandly anyway.** The Crofters' Act 1886 ("the
  Magna Carta of Gaeldom") secured *tenancy*, not ownership, and pointedly refused to return
  cleared land. The name carries built-in humility: a charter against arbitrary eviction is
  not the same as owning the commons, and the difference is where the next fight lives.

- **The truest modern echo was community ownership, not individual right-to-buy.** Assynt
  (1993) and Eigg (1997), not the hedged 1976 individual buyout. Design instruction: the
  meaningful unit is the community holding the commons together, not each user getting a deed
  to a tiny plot. Atomised ownership can be a consolation prize that leaves the structure
  intact.

- **The maintenance burden transfers cleanly.** Modern crofting's problem is the inverse of
  1886: not eviction but absenteeism, neglect, depopulation — hence residency and
  use-it-or-lose-it duties. Any commons (physical or virtual) inherits this: securing the
  commons is the *start* of the maintenance problem, not the end. This is the "maintenance
  phase" question the dossier raised, imported honestly.

- **The lineage gives weight without overclaiming.** Winstanley's "common treasury" (1649,
  crushed within a year, still a reference point), the Irish Land League, the Lowland
  Levellers, Ostrom's governable commons vs. Hardin's myth — the pattern is that "improvement"
  framed as economic necessity is the recurring language under which collectively-held space
  gets privately captured, and people keep contesting it with the same moral claim. Enclosure
  of physical commons → IP enclosure → platform enclosure of social/informational space.

## The honest disanalogy (keep it visible)

Land is rivalrous and finite; virtual space is closer to non-rivalrous and reproducible,
which changes the economics of "enclosure" — you can fork, you can exit; the lock-in is
network effects and data gravity, not physical scarcity. Ostrom's principles were derived
from finite common-pool resources and don't all port. The strongest, most crofting-accurate
posture is to name which parts of the pattern we claim (engineered dependency, collective
tenure, the maintenance burden) and be candid about where the land analogy stops carrying
weight.

# Brand and Voice Notes (working draft)

A working draft, not settled. Captures the brand thinking so far so we can reflect
on it and keep refining. This is the rough shape; much is still being turned over.
Nothing here is committed copy.

Paired with `design-philosophy.md` (the why), `design-criteria.md` (the how/the
bar), and the natural-world vocabulary (ponds, pads, Croft). This doc is the
voice and message layer that sits on top of those.

---

## The name

**Croft.** A croft is a small enclosed plot of worked land, a homestead patch you
tend yourself. It sits naturally in the same neighborhood as garden, plot, and
swimming pond, all small, owned, cultivated, warm, human-scale places. The name
carries the whole ethos quietly: small, yours, tended, not industrial.

---

## The core idea the brand has to convey

A place you actually own and shape, warm and low-pressure, where you assemble your
own social world from the parts you want and ignore the rest. Composability as a
user-respecting value. Not extractive. Tended, not manufactured.

## The tagline

**"Grow your own."**

Why it works:

- It is active, an invitation to do something, not a passive description.

- It is ownable and anti-platform: grow your *own*, you are not renting someone
else's feed.

- It carries a warm, homegrown, faintly countercultural, made-not-manufactured
connotation that fits the non-extractive stance.

- It is garden-native without invoking water, so it does not force the
garden-versus-pond metaphor reconciliation. It sits in the loose natural
neighborhood without demanding rigid metaphorical consistency.

- It provokes the productive question "own what?", which the brand then answers
(see below). A good tagline opens a small curiosity gap rather than closing
everything.

Minor caution to be aware of, not blocking: "grow your own" has a mild
home-cultivation (incl. cannabis) association in some contexts. Usually harmless,
even endearing-rebellious, and on-tone. Know it is there.

### The static form

**"Croft. Grow your own."** with the period and the implied blank.

The incompleteness is deliberate: the trailing "own ___" invites the reader to
mentally fill the blank, which provokes "own what?" and whose honest answer is
"whatever you want, that is the point." The implied-blank version is likely the
most elegant static lockup (logo, favicon, merch) precisely because it cannot
rotate but still carries the invitation.

A first wordmark exploration lives at `../../assets/croft-wordmark-pastoral.png` (see
`../../assets/README.md`): "CROFT" with a pastoral crofting scene — grass, wildflowers, a
winding stream and footpath — rendered inside the letterforms. It puts the crofting metaphor
(tended smallholding, local-first, "grow your own") directly in the type. A draft direction to
refine, not a settled mark.

### The dynamic / rotating form

**"Grow your own ___"** with the blank cycling through a curated set.

This is a rotating (enumerated) tagline: a fixed stem plus a changing completion.
The key insight: the *variety itself is the message*. A tagline that can hold many
endings demonstrates composability rather than describing it. The form embodies
the thesis, the medium is the message. For a composable, you-shape-it product this
is the single most on-thesis tagline structure available.

It answers "own what?" by demonstration rather than by one noun (which would shrink
the product) or a manifesto (which would lecture): watch, a different answer each
time, you decide, the variety is the answer.

Execution discipline (a real failure mode): the rotation needs a stable anchor
("grow your own", always present) and a curated, vetted completion set where every
entry is genuinely true of the product and genuinely felt as un-owned elsewhere.
The moment a completion is a stretch ("grow your own synergy"), it cheapens the
whole device. Curate the rotation set the way pads are curated: small, vetted,
every entry earns its place.

Note: a rotating tagline needs a medium that can rotate (a site where the word
changes, varied social bios, merch with different completions) to be fully alive.
In static contexts, use the default rest state or the implied-blank form.

### The default rest state

**"Grow your own community."**

Community is the truest single completion, the emotional center. Underneath the
feeds, pads, and games, the thing is people connecting (Croft, chat, the shared
garden). So community is not just one rotation option; it is where the variety
rests, the home-page landing, the loop-closer. It "bubbles up" because it is the
heart the variety orbits, not merely a member of the set.

### The rotation set (to be curated, draft candidates)

Completions should be true of the product and felt as un-owned on extractive
platforms. Draft pool, not finalized: community, feed, space, rules, quiet, fun,
world. The strongest are the ones that name something the user quietly knows
mainstream social took from them (your feed is the algorithm's, your attention is
harvested, your data is the product). Still to do: nail the small final set; the
wrong word cheapens it, the right small set makes it sing.

## Answering "own what?" (the two-speed answer)

The tagline provokes "own what?", which is really two questions: the surface one
("your own *what* noun") and the deep one ("in what sense *own*"). The answer works
at two speeds, matching follow-or-ignore.

- **The easy answer** (for someone who just wants to know what they get): plain,
concrete, warm. Something like "your feeds, your people, a space that is actually
yours." Tells the nouns and moves on. Grandma gets it. This stays easy; it must
never become a manifesto at the front door.

- **The deep answer** (for whoever leans in and means "own" philosophically): the
quiet realization that mainstream social owns all of this *for* you and *against*
you (the feed to keep you scrolling, the attention to harvest, the data as the
product), and that you currently own none of it. Croft hands it back. This is the
cooperative, non-extractive thesis, available to the curious, never forced.

Discipline: the easy answer stays easy; the depth stays available, never
mandatory. The whole front-door interaction is "Grow your own" -> "your feeds,
your people, a space that is actually yours." Everything else waits for whoever
looks.

## "Homegrown" (the identity word, different register)

**"Homegrown"** does a different job than the tagline and belongs in different
rooms.

- "Grow your own" is an *imperative*, user-facing, asks the user to participate.

- "Homegrown" is an *adjective/identity*, describes what Croft *is*: made with
care, local-first, not venture-extractive, the opposite of "platform."

Use "homegrown" when talking *about* the project (about page, the ethos, merch,
the voice describing Croft itself), and "grow your own" when talking *to* the user
(the invitation). Two registers, both true, different rooms.

## Other lines considered (and their right roles)

These came up and each has a role, even the ones not chosen as the lead:

- **"Social without the noise."** The most immediately legible to a stranger;
names a real pain cleanly. But it is defined by negation (what we are *not*), which
is fragile and slightly more corporate-clever than the homegrown voice. Role: a
clear one-line explainer for skeptical newcomers, not the soul of the brand.

- **"A warm, sunny place."** Pleasant and on-palette, but a *mood*, not a *promise*;
interchangeable. Role: supporting atmosphere/subhead, lives well *under* a stronger
line, not as the lead.

- **"A cooperative experience."** The most values-honest, strong for the
co-op/commons-minded audience, but reads vague or institutional to a general
audience. Role: the deeper values/trust layer (a reason to stay and trust), not the
front-door hook.

- **"Tending to the heart."** Lovely "tending" energy (on-metaphor), but "the
heart" drifts wellness-coded and overpromises relative to a social client. Role:
the warmth of "tending" is worth keeping in the voice; this exact line probably
not.

- **"Come on in, the water's fine."** Charming, warm, lowers the barrier (an
invitation, ideal at thresholds/onboarding and rhyming with the games hook, a
friendly "jump in and play"). Two cautions: it is very American-colloquial (does
not travel/translate well), and it invokes water where the tagline invokes garden
(needs the ponds-in-a-garden image to reconcile). Role: a warm domestic-context
invitation at onboarding thresholds, not the globally-portable lead. The garden
tagline travels far better than the water line.

## How it all layers (the message funnel)

The lines are not competitors; they are layers of one funnel, and the layering is
itself on-thesis (follow-or-ignore applied to the brand):

- **Identity slot** (always with the name): Croft. Grow your own.

- **Invitation, dynamic**: Grow your own ___ (rotating), resting on "community."

- **Easy explainer** (what is this): your feeds, your people, a space that is
actually yours. (Or the punchier "social without the noise" for skeptics.)

- **Atmosphere/mood**: warm, sunny, cultivated, homegrown; the cream-green-earth
world.

- **Identity word, about-the-project**: Homegrown.

- **Deep values, for the curious**: cooperative, member-tended, non-extractive;
you own none of this elsewhere, here you do.

The surface is a warm three-word invitation; the curious can follow it down to the
explainer, the mood, the identity, and finally the cooperative values. The brand
practices the product's own principle: calm surface, depth on demand, nothing
forced.

## On the metaphor neighborhood (a deliberate looseness)

Croft, plot, garden, swimming pond, and "grow your own" all live in the same warm
natural neighborhood, but the brand deliberately does *not* enforce rigid
single-metaphor consistency. Loose metaphorical kinship reads as a rich world;
rigid consistency reads as a gimmick. The architecture quietly reconciles the
garden-and-water images anyway (ponds are bodies of water *within* the garden; pads
are lily pads floating in it), so the images cohere without being forced. Keep the
neighborhood warm and loose, not locked.

Watch-item: do not over-tighten the metaphor until it turns against itself, and do
not let the American water idiom carry weight it cannot bear globally. The garden
imperative ("grow your own") is the portable core; the rest is warm local color.

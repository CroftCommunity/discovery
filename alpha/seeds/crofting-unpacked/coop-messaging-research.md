# Cooperative messaging and market research — an evidence base for the Croft voice registry

Provenance: researched and written 2026-07-16/19 in working sessions with Claude
(web sources retrieved those days; URLs in the reference list). This document is
the evidence base sitting behind the voice registry in brand-and-voice; the
registry states the rules, this states why. Suggested home:
`beta/socialization/coop-messaging-research.md`.

## 1. Why co-ops are the reference class

Croft's stack has three layers with three different ownership claims: the
Drystone protocol is no one's to own; a person's records are theirs alone; and
Croft, one infrastructure deployment of the protocol, is intended to be run by
a cooperative its members own. That third layer makes the cooperative movement
the closest living tradition: a century-plus of organizations that are
member-owned, non-extractive by structure, and forced to communicate warmth
and ownership against better-funded incumbents. Their messaging successes are
templates; their documented struggles are forewarnings; and one rule falls out
before any line does: **ownership language must be layer-scoped.** "No one
owns the protocol. You own your records. Members own the co-op." Blending the
layers produces claims that are either false or unfalsifiable.

## 2. Agricultural co-ops: identity, inheritance, and scale drift

Dairy Farmers of America (Kansas City, MO; among the largest co-ops in the
country) repeats "farmer-owned" as an identity prefix rather than a feature,
and distills its mission to delivering value to members. Cabot compresses the
same move best: "We're not just Cabot farmers, we're owners." Tillamook adds
the permanence vow: "a farmer-owned co-op. Always has been. Always will be."
DFA and Tillamook both lean on inheritance — passing farms and legacies to
the next generation — confirming that "handed down" is load-bearing across
century-old co-op messaging.

Two forewarnings ride along. Tillamook was sued over its "Dairy Done Right"
campaign, the complaint alleging small-farm imagery misled consumers about
actual sourcing: warm ownership messaging invites audit, and a co-op can fail
the audit when boards and supply chains sit between claim and member. And a
labor-federation profile of DFA records member farmers complaining that
financial gains sat with executives and business partners and that they were
not informed how co-op money was spent: at sufficient scale, "member-owned"
stays legally true while becoming emotionally false.

The counter-pattern is MFA Incorporated (Columbia, MO), whose messaging still
leads with seven farmers in a one-room schoolhouse in 1914 ordering 1,150
pounds of baler twine and saving $400. The tiny concrete origin story outworks
every adjective a century later. MFA also quietly re-expanded its acronym from
Missouri Farmers Association to "Made for Agriculture" — a living Missouri
precedent for the acronym-re-expansion move ENS makes.

Lessons: ownership as identity prefix; the permanence vow form; inheritance
language; concrete origin stories over adjectives; warm claims must be
auditable and stay at a scale where they remain felt.

## 3. Credit unions: the awareness paradox and the common-bond paradox

The credit union movement's defining modern struggle is documented by its own
people. Market share sat at roughly 7% for 25 years despite a positive
reputation; the research behind CUNA's "Open Your Eyes to a Credit Union"
campaign (launched January 2019) found consumers familiar with credit unions
but unsure they were eligible to join and assuming their money would be hard
to access. State-league figures: 98% of non-members have heard of credit
unions; fewer than 14% can recall one as an option. Banks outspend credit
unions on marketing roughly $43 to $1. Three years and a $100M commitment in,
consumer consideration rose materially (checking 13%→19%, lending 14%→22%)
while membership declined in about half the campaign states, with joining
tools still "in planning." By 2024, per NCUA data cited by Filene, 56% of all
credit unions lost members.

Filene Research Institute (the movement's research body) adds the sharpest
findings. Its 2026 benchmarking finds leader optimism about younger members
"does not align with reality," names "quiet churn" (dormant accounts while
real activity happens at fintechs), and identifies awareness itself as the
bottleneck — many young people have simply never heard of credit unions, and
their comparison set is Delta, Airbnb, Venmo, and Amazon, where dated systems
read as dated thinking. Filene's marketing center opens on the right
corrective for any registry: storytelling cannot compensate for an undefined
value proposition. And its FiLab tested influencer partnerships across 13
credit unions, finding trusted-creator campaigns moved awareness and
engagement dramatically — trust transfers through people, not ads.

The finding with the deepest Croft implication: Filene attributes part of the
membership decline to **expanded fields of membership**, which removed the
built-in, well-defined audiences. The common bond was simultaneously the gate
(feeding the fatal "I can't join" myth) and the identity (the reason a
teachers' credit union felt like yours). Dissolving the gate to grow dissolved
the bond. Croft's layer stack resolves the paradox structurally: the network
is open to everyone at the protocol layer, while real bonds live at community
scale. Open gate and common bond, at different layers.

Lessons: perceived gates kill co-ops faster than dislike; awareness without a
doorstep does not convert (every message keeps a next step attached); you
cannot outshout at 43:1, only out-true; ownership must be translated into a
receipt ("when you become a member, you become a shareholder," with billions
returned) — and for a Drystone-based co-op the receipt is native: governance
lineage on the era spine is the patronage refund's informational equivalent,
ownership a member can check rather than take on faith.

## 4. Federation branding: Touchstone Energy

Touchstone Energy is the national brand of the electric cooperative network:
local co-ops keep their own names and identities while the shared mark
certifies values and network membership to some 32 million member-owners,
with locals reassuring members "your co-op is still your co-op." The tagline
"The Power of Human Connections" was selected after surveying thousands of
co-op members because it portrayed the relationship value of cooperative
service; the brand book glosses it as "We are owners; we are all connected;
we are all one." This is the brand architecture for a future of many
Drystone-running infrastructure co-ops: protocol-level trust mark, local
identity preserved, Croft as the first local.

One inversion available to Croft: Touchstone's warmth line is a federally
registered trademark with mandatory ® usage. A Croft voice registry published
under a free license — warmth explicitly meant to be copied — states the
difference better than any line in it.

## 5. Platform cooperatives: the direct equivalents

The nearest kin are platform co-ops — the movement framed by Trebor Scholz
(The New School) asking why platform owners and investors must be the main
beneficiaries of platform-based labor. Documented examples and mechanics:

- **Stocksy United** (Victoria, BC; founded by iStock's founders after its
  sale to Getty): artist-owned stock photography; members receive 50% of
  royalties (90% on extended licenses) and share year-end surplus; revenues
  of $3.7M by 2014 and $10.7M by 2016 with millions paid out in artist
  surplus; multi-stakeholder classes with an online resolution process for
  member votes. The movement's flagship proof that a co-op platform can be
  profitable and generous simultaneously.
- **Up & Go** (cleaning): 95% of job fees flow to worker-owners. **The
  Drivers Cooperative** (NYC ride-hail): 70% of net profits distributed to
  drivers via patronage points tracking ride volume. Patronage made legible —
  the felt-ownership mechanism in working form.
- **Resonate** (music streaming): multi-stakeholder (artists, listeners,
  workers) with a stream-to-own model — each play costs a little more until
  the listener owns the track, converting engagement into ownership rather
  than into ad inventory.
- **CoopCycle**: a federation of 60+ local courier co-ops across 11 countries
  — the Touchstone pattern re-emerging natively digital: shared software and
  mark, local ownership.
- Honest movement-wide assessment, from its own literature: most examples are
  "brave experiments at best"; the successes are real but niche, and none has
  displaced a dominant incumbent. The standing structural constraint is
  capitalization — a co-op cannot sell control to venture capital, so it
  cannot buy growth, which is also precisely why it cannot be made to rot on
  an investor's schedule.

The single closest equivalent to a Croft co-op is **social.coop**: a
cooperatively run Mastodon instance founded in 2017 out of the #BuyTwitter
moment, with platform-co-op movement co-founder Nathan Schneider among its
founders. Members pay a sliding-scale contribution (£1–£10/month), govern via
Loomio proposals, and staff working groups for community moderation (with
moderator stipends) and tech operations. It self-describes, accurately, as a
"cooperatively-run, long-running, stable provider of social media," and
publishes a guide for starting fediverse co-ops ("How to Make the Fediverse
Your Own"). Co-founder Mayel de Borniol's founding diagnosis is the entire
Croft infra thesis in one sentence: the fediverse risked "simply moving from
oligarchy to feudalism," with no accountability for node admins, and the
answer was a participatory, democratic, transparent model for organizing
nodes, scaled horizontally.

Instructive contrast: Mozilla.social, a corporation's Mastodon instance,
announced shutdown in 2024 with 270 active users — a mission-driven company
exited social infrastructure that a small member-funded co-op has sustained
for years. Institutional form predicted persistence better than institutional
size.

## 6. Similar but not the same

Shared with credit unions and ag co-ops: the awareness bottleneck, the
felt-ownership problem, out-true-don't-outshout economics, the inheritance
and permanence registers, the federation-mark opportunity.

Different for platform and social-infrastructure co-ops, and for Croft:

1. **Network effects replace field-of-membership as the gate.** A credit
   union's obstacle was "I can't join"; a social platform's is "nobody I know
   is there." Croft inherits ATProto's answer: the graph lives at the
   protocol layer, so joining Croft never means leaving anyone.
2. **Governance is labor.** social.coop's stipended moderation and volunteer
   working groups show the real recurring cost of a social co-op is human
   attention, not servers. A Croft co-op budget should assume it.
3. **Capitalization constraints bind harder in tech** (build costs precede
   revenue), but Croft's backendless, static-first architecture is unusually
   cheap to operate — the design already fits the funding form.
4. **The co-op does not have to be immortal.** This is Croft's structural
   novelty over every equivalent: with records at the protocol layer, an
   infrastructure co-op that fails is an inconvenience, not a bulldozed
   neighborhood. The co-op only has to be good, never too big to fail. No
   platform co-op running its own proprietary stack can say that; it is the
   strongest honest claim available to a Croft co-op and belongs in its
   founding documents.

## 7. Distilled rules (feeding the voice registry)

1. Ownership language layer-scoped, always.
2. Ownership as identity prefix; permanence as vow; inheritance as register.
3. Concrete origin stories over adjectives.
4. Warm claims stay auditable and human-scale; publish the receipt (era-spine
   governance lineage as the native patronage statement).
5. No perceived gates, ever; open gate at protocol layer, real bonds at
   community layer.
6. Every message carries its doorstep.
7. Out-true rather than outshout; neighbor-to-neighbor trust transfer is the
   mechanism, not the fallback.
8. Free-license the registry itself.

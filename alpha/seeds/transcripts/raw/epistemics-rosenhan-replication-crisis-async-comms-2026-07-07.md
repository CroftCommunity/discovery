## Rosenhan and the anti-psychiatry context

- **Rosenhan, "On Being Sane in Insane Places" (Science, 1973):** eight healthy pseudopatients presented
  at 12 psychiatric hospitals in 5 states claiming to hear "empty," "hollow," "thud"; once admitted they
  behaved normally, were never detected as sane, and were released as "schizophrenia in remission." Note-
  taking was recorded by staff as pathology ("engages in writing behavior"); 35 of 118 actual patients in
  the first three hospitalizations voiced suspicion the pseudopatients were not real patients.

- **Anti-psychiatry movement (1960s-70s)** provided the cultural fuel: Thomas Szasz, The Myth of Mental
  Illness (1961), argued mental illness is a social/legal construct enforcing conformity; R.D. Laing. The
  study "proved" the establishment was incompetent or oppressive, which is why its methodological flaws
  were ignored, the narrative fit the intellectual appetite of the time (Rosenhan's data and the sentiment
  were codependent).

- **The later critique [CONTESTED]:** Susannah Cahalan (2019) argued Rosenhan likely fabricated or
  distorted data (pseudopatient accounts conflicted with hospital records). So the 1973 narrative is now
  under intense scrutiny for factual integrity.

## The collapse of verification latency (the load-bearing epistemics point)

- In 1973 the barrier to challenging such a study was huge: you needed the unpublished files, the
  anonymous subjects, and cross-state record audits. A reader could not sanity-check the data; authority
  rested on the journal's and researcher's prestige (trust-the-expert).

- Today verification cost is near zero: a significant paper enters a decentralized, global "lab" of
  critics; thin data or suspect methods surface on public forums (e.g. PubPeer) in days/weeks, not 40
  years. We moved from "trust the expert" to a system designed to stress-test claims in real time. Side
  effect: truth *feels* more fragile because we watch the demolition in progress; faking did not
  necessarily increase, detection caught up to the frequency of publication.

- **The replication crisis** is the institutional/systemic version: an acknowledgment that the way science
  was conducted and vetted for decades was structurally incapable of ensuring its results were true. Split
  pre/post-internet: pre-internet, gatekeepers (journals, tenure, editors) were the only filters and the
  crisis could not fully manifest because decentralized real-time auditing did not exist; post-internet,
  once raw datasets were shared digitally, the gap between the published narrative and the underlying
  evidence became visible to anyone with a browser. The crisis was the infrastructure catching up to the
  claims.

## The generational divide in inputs (the user's framing)

The "great acceleration": before ~150 years ago, change came in ~1000-year increments; then it started
accelerating. Developing the tools to navigate the present requires either being born into it or working
hard at it, which relates to a perceived generational divide in *where inputs come from*. Less about age
than the cognitive environment of one's formative years: those formed when information was scarce/expensive
default to an *authority-based* heuristic (if it is in print or from an institution, presume true); those
formed when information is abundant/cheap need an *adversarial* heuristic (if presented as true, interrogate
and cross-reference). Thinking adversarially without becoming cynical is a manual override of the
consensus-trusting brain, learned rather than inherited.

## The tail-risk the user named

"Epistemic bubbles": the problem moved from "not enough data" to "too much data that fits our priors."
Having the tools to navigate information is only half the battle; the other half is the discipline not to
use those tools to merely harden existing beliefs.

## Design relevance (why this is philosophy/epistemics for Drystone)

This is the epistemic case under Part 1's "no center can hold the truth" and "compute provenance, never
utility": institutional, closed-loop authority let flawed/fabricated data survive precisely because
verification was centralized and high-latency; a provenance-first, locally-verifiable, content-addressed
substrate (where a claim carries its own auditable chain rather than resting on a center's prestige) is the
structural answer. It also tempers the design's own optimism: adversarial verification is powerful but
insufficient against tribal/ideological priors (see the Lifeworld transcript), so provenance must be paired
with real-humanity grounding, not treated as a cure by itself.

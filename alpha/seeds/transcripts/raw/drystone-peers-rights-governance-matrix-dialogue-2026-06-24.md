**ASSISTANT:** You're not wrong, and the diagnosis is mostly structural rather than a failure of imagination.

- *The lock-in story is real but incomplete.* iMessage's "broken cross compatibility" is deliberate: green bubbles are a retention moat, and internal Apple emails surfaced during the Epic litigation showed executives explicitly rejecting an Android iMessage client because it would "remove [an] obstacle to iPhone families giving their kids Android phones." So at least one of your suspicions is documented fact, not speculation. But lock-in alone doesn't explain why even the privacy-good, no-ad options (Signal) feel rigid. That points at something deeper.
- *The architectural original sin.* Almost every mainstream messenger models a conversation as a **room with a fixed membership and an append-only log**. The room is the primary object. That single choice produces every symptom: groups can't split or merge because identity is the room, not the people; attachments are second-class (payloads stapled to a message, not addressable objects, so you "recapture rather than reference"); search/export are bad because the log is optimized for delivery and ordering, not retrieval. What you're describing (peers as the durable entity, conversations as overlapping waves, external assets as referenceable) is closer to a content-addressed, capability-based model than a room-based one.
- *Why the better model doesn't ship:* E2E encryption fights referenceability (only endpoints can read → no server-side index/addressing); sync engines are brutally hard (multi-device offline-first mergeable state is a CRDT problem most teams won't take on); no business model rewards portability (export/reference are anti-retention).
- *Where you might be slightly wrong:* much is hard rather than neglected. Matrix actually does model rooms as referenceable objects with a shared event graph and federation, the closest mainstream attempt, but the lived experience is sluggish and the UX rough — which tells you the hard part isn't the idea. Given your Croft/Drystone work the relevant prior art is the iroh/Willow lineage you're already in.
- Net: ~40% deliberate moats, ~40% genuine unsolved privacy-vs-referenceable-state tension, ~20% path dependence on a delivery-reliability data model.

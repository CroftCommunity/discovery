## The research narrative (verbatim)

A note before the narrative: the popular version runs roughly "ancient Highlanders lived
freely on communal land for centuries, were brutally cleared by greedy landlords, fought
back heroically, and won their land back in 1886." Almost every clause needs qualifying. The
communal system was older than crofting but crofting itself was largely invented by the same
landlords who did the clearing. The 1886 Act was a real, hard-won victory, but it secured
tenancy, not ownership, and pointedly refused to give back the cleared land. And the
resistance, though genuine, has been romanticized in ways that flatten its actual
organization and limits.

**1. What a croft actually is.** A croft is "a small piece of land entirely surrounded by
legislation." Not a house — a small piece of agricultural land (a micro-farm), with a strict
legal definition; the cottage is incidental, the croft is the land and the tenancy.
Historically crofts were tenancies: rent to a landlord, but can't be put off the land while
working it and paying, and can pass the tenancy to family. A township = a cluster of these,
with individual arable "inbye" plus shared "common grazing" — dual structure, individual
enterprise plus communal working. The communal, hereditary relationship predates crofting:
the Gaelic *dùthchas*, a collective heritage guaranteeing the people's hereditary stake in
clan territories as common property; below the chief sat the *tacksman* who sublet. The croft
system was built on the wreckage of that world: crofting communities were a product of the
Clearances, replacing the *bailtean*. Landlords eliminated tacksmen to capture their cut
(Devine: the death of old Gaelic society), and the new crofts were designed too small to live
on — a contemporary estimate held a crofter needed 200 days of work away from the croft to
avoid destitution. That was deliberate: a half-starved tenant is a captive labour force
(kelp). Crofting in origin was less a peasant idyll than a labour-tying arrangement dressed as
land reform. (Read: Devine, *The Scottish Clearances* (2018), *Clanship to Crofters' War*;
Hunter, *The Making of the Crofting Community* (1976).)

**2. The Highland Clearances.** Sheep paid better than people; the incomers weren't all
foreigners (over half the Sutherland sheep leases went to Sutherlanders). Two phases. First
(late 18thC–1820s): keep people as coastal kelp/fishing labour — Sutherland, ~15,000
relocated c.1809–21, James Loch organizing, Patrick Sellar the face (acquitted of culpable
homicide 1816; roof timbers burned so people couldn't return). Sellar's justification
("barbarous Highlanders... advance in civilisation") vs. Donald McLeod's eyewitness *Gloomy
Memories*. Second phase (from 1840s): remove them — potato famine + kelp collapse. Unlike
Ireland, government/charity/diaspora relief averted Irish-scale starvation, though
administered on hard free-market principles. Scale (contested): 200,000+ forced off
1770s–1850s, 70,000+ emigrated; the Highland and Island Emigration Society assisted ~5,000 to
Australia 1852–57. Resist the cartoon villain even while keeping moral weight: John Gordon of
Cluny (Tobago plantations, compensated for ~1,400 enslaved people in 1833; Barra 1851), but
the Barra forced-emigration story is where oral tradition and the documentary record diverge.
(Read: Richards, *Debating the Highland Clearances*; Devine (2018); Prebble (1963, dramatized);
McLeod, *Gloomy Memories*; the Angus Macleod Archive.)

**3. The resistance.** 1880s; Skye the crucible. Battle of the Braes (April 1882): crofters
refused rent until grazing on Ben Lee restored; forced a sheriff's officer to burn eviction
notices; 50 Glasgow police repelled by ~100 men/women/children; the state sent the gunboat
*Jackal*. Glendale produced John MacPherson, the literate, bilingual "Skye Martyr," jailed;
~400 marines stationed on Skye Nov 1884. Tactics — rent strikes and land raids — borrowed
from Ireland; the Highland Land League consciously modelled on the Irish Land League.
Journalist John Murdoch (*The Highlander*) seeded the politics. Precision point: there was no
organized "Highland Land League" before 1885 (the name was adopted 14 Sept 1887); the early
risings were local and spontaneous. (Read: I. M. M. MacPhail, *The Crofters' War*; Hunter.)

**4. The political path to law.** Napier Commission (heard evidence 1883; report April 1884):
800 witnesses; first was Angus Stewart of Braes, in Gaelic, asking simply to "give us land."
The report disappointed — landowner-packed, it would grant security only to ~10% (holdings ≥£6
rent), fearing to "perpetuate social evils of a dangerous character," and recommended the
poorest (<£6/yr) be emigrated. The 1884 franchise extension let crofters vote; Crofters' Party
MPs elected 1885 in Argyll, Inverness, Ross & Cromarty, Caithness. Gladstone set Napier's
scheme aside and passed the **Crofters' Holdings (Scotland) Act 1886**, modelled on the Irish
Land Acts: security of tenure, compensation for improvements, right to bequeath, right to
renounce, fair rent fixed every 7 years; first Crofters Commission as a land court. What it
left unsolved: it did **not** return cleared land — overcrowding and landlessness persisted;
it entrenched fragmentation; landlords kept reserved rights (mining, shooting, access,
resumption). Some historians: it created an archaic frozen tenancy, where Ireland moved
tenants toward outright ownership. "Magna Carta of Gaeldom" (John Lorne Campbell) — apt
precisely as the original Magna Carta was: a landmark limit on arbitrary power that fell well
short of liberation, its symbolic afterlife outgrowing its text. (Read: the Act at
legislation.gov.uk; Napier report + evidence; Cameron, *Land for the People?*)

**5. Aftermath and long arc.** Land raids continued (Vatersay 1906); 1911 Small Landholders
Act; 1919 Land Settlement Act (state buys land, by compulsion, to resettle ex-servicemen).
The Leverhulme/Lewis episode (1917–23) is the best honest counterpoint: the soap magnate
bought Lewis/Harris, proposed an industrial-fishing future, and was refused by returning
ex-servicemen (raw from WWI losses + the *Iolaire* disaster) who wanted the land they'd been
promised — at Gress Bridge he stood on a barrel and said "No, I will not give you the land..."
The crofters weren't merely sentimental: they knew crofting+fishing were complementary
insurance against each other's failure; his schemes then collapsed on commodity prices +
Lever Brothers' troubles. The 1976 Crofting Act gave the right to buy (15 years' rent), but
hedged (lost grants; Land Court could refuse). The truest modern echo was **community
ownership**: Assynt (1993, 21,000 acres), Eigg (1997); the Land Reform (Scotland) Act 2003
gave crofting communities a near-absolute right to buy. Did the win hold? Partly — crofting
survives (~770,000 ha, ~25% of agricultural land in the crofting counties), but the modern
problem is the inverse of 1886: not eviction but absenteeism, neglect, depopulation (South
Uist 6,000 in 1883 → <2,000), hence residency/use-it duties; overlaid by a rural housing
crisis and extreme land concentration (>50% of Scotland owned by ~432 landowners). (Read:
Hunter, *From the Low Tide of the Sea...*; Hutchinson, *The Soap Man*; Wightman, *The Poor
Had No Lawyers*; Community Land Scotland.)

**6. The deeper analog: commons and enclosure.** English enclosure is the master case: >7
million acres enclosed 1760–1870 via ~4,000 Acts, privatising nearly half of Britain's land;
the effect was dispossession that produces wage labour (Polanyi's "Great Transformation").
The deepest counter-claim: the **Diggers / True Levellers** (1649), Gerrard Winstanley — the
earth as "a common treasury of relief for all, both beasts and men"; dug St George's Hill;
argued William the Conqueror "turned the English out of their birthrights." Crushed within a
year; "common treasury" became a permanent reference point. The intellectual fight: Hardin's
1968 "Tragedy of the Commons" vs. Elinor Ostrom (Törbel alpine commons, stably managed for
centuries) — historians call Hardin bad history: the English commons weren't overused but
*stolen* via enclosure. E. P. Thompson's "moral economy" is the bridge. The pattern recurs:
Ireland (Land League → peasant proprietorship, a more complete outcome than Scotland's); the
Lowland Scottish "Levellers" of the 1720s (pulled down enclosure dykes); Winstanley → Spence →
Paine → modern community-land/food-sovereignty. Common thread: "improvement" framed as
economic necessity is the recurring language under which collectively-held land is
privatized, contested with the same moral claim — the land is a common inheritance, not a
commodity. (Read: Winstanley, *The True Levellers Standard Advanced*; Hill, *The World Turned
Upside Down*; Ostrom, *Governing the Commons*; Thompson, *Customs in Common*; Neeson,
*Commoners*; Polanyi.)

**7. Honest counterpoints.** The "ancient free crofters" myth (crofts were engineered
undersized holdings); the "noble chief betrayed by outsiders" myth (much clearance was
Scot-on-Scot, kin-on-kin); the genocide framing is genuinely contested (Devine: "ludicrous to
suggest a genocide... no direct evidence of increasing mortality caused by clearances," Highland
population actually rose 1801–1850s; others hold "ethnic cleansing" fits the deliberate
removal — a live argument among serious people); Prebble's popular account treated with caution;
Highland exceptionalism (the Lowlands/Borders were cleared too, often earlier); the
"league-led heroic campaign" myth; the 1886 "victory" myth (tenancy not land); and the
Leverhulme ambiguity (the refusal was a defensible risk calculation, not simple heroism or
backwardness). To argue from the contested parts: Richards, *Debating the Highland Clearances*;
Devine, *The Scottish Clearances*; Hunter, *The Making of the Crofting Community*.

## The naming argument (verbatim)

> **I actually think all that makes the case of usage as a name of the eternal struggle coop
> for virtual space more powerful**

It does, and the reason is worth being precise about, because the precision is what protects
the name from the romanticization you spent this whole inquiry trying to strip away.

The weak version: "crofters fought landlords for their land, we're fighting platform owners
for our digital space, same eternal struggle." That's the tartan-tinged version Devine would
puncture in a sentence.

The strong version, which your research actually supports, is stronger *because* it's less
heroic:

- **The croft was engineered dispossession dressed as tenure.** Holdings deliberately sized
  too small to live on, so the crofter sold labour to the same party that controlled the land.
  That is the exact shape of a user's relationship with a platform: just enough space to be
  present, never enough to be independent, the deficit filled by working for the landlord
  (attention, content, data as the kelp). Naming a co-op "Croft" names the *trap* — a
  permanent reminder of the default arrangement.

- **The win was real but partial, and named grandly anyway.** "Magna Carta of Gaeldom" for an
  Act that secured tenancy and refused to give back the land. Carried honestly, the name
  builds in its own humility: a charter against arbitrary eviction is not owning the commons,
  and the difference is where the next fight lives.

- **The truest modern echo was community ownership, not individual right-to-buy.** Assynt and
  Eigg, not the hedged 1976 Act. Design instruction: the meaningful unit is the community
  holding the land in common, not each user getting a deed to their plot. Atomised ownership
  can be a consolation prize that leaves the structure intact.

- **The unresolved tension transfers cleanly.** Modern crofting's problem is the inverse of
  1886: not eviction but absenteeism, neglect, depopulation — the residency/use-it duties. Any
  co-op for virtual space inherits exactly this: securing the commons is the *start* of the
  maintenance problem, not the end. That's the "maintenance phase" question, imported honestly.

- **The deeper lineage gives weight without overclaiming.** Winstanley's "common treasury"
  (1649, crushed within a year, still a reference point); the pattern across Diggers, Irish
  Land League, Lowland Levellers, Assynt is that "improvement" framed as economic necessity is
  the recurring language under which collectively-held space gets privately captured. Enclosure
  of physical commons → IP enclosure → platform enclosure of social/informational space.

So the name earns its power by pointing at the unglamorous parts: the croft as a labour trap,
the charter as an incomplete win, ownership as something that must be collective and then
actively maintained or it rots.

One honest caution: the disanalogies are real. Land is rivalrous and finite; virtual space is
closer to non-rivalrous and reproducible, which changes the economics of "enclosure" (you can
fork, you can exit; the lock-in is network effects and data gravity, not physical scarcity).
Ostrom's principles were derived from finite common-pool resources and don't all port. The
strongest version names which parts of the croft pattern it's claiming (engineered dependency,
collective tenure, the maintenance burden) and is candid about where the land analogy stops
carrying weight. That candor is itself the most crofting-accurate thing you could do.

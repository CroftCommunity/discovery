# The historical lineage of peer rights — devices, networks, and the one boundary

date: 2026-06-26

status: thinking (new) — a historical-alignment companion, deliberately separate from the
reasoning-foundations. It records a *historical precedent* for the principle that a participant may use
its own device on a shared network on its own terms, so long as it does not break the network for others.
This is **about peer rights in general, not about any one ecosystem** (Croft included); they are closely
related but should not be conflated.

## The boundary it grounds

The principle: a participant may use its own device, and arrange its own affairs, in ways that are
**privately beneficial without being publicly detrimental** — and only *public detriment* (breaking the
shared network, removing others' standing) is regulable. Private benefit (your tool, your data, your
exit) is protected. This is the historical, legal form of "no right to remove the rights of others": the
one boundary is about *public detriment*, not private arrangement.

## The confirmed ancestor

> a user may use a device "in ways which are privately beneficial without being publicly detrimental."
> — Judge Bazelon, *Hush-A-Phone v. United States* (1956)

*Verification:* **CONFIRMED** (Bazelon authored the standard).

The *Hush-A-Phone* was a cup-shaped device that clipped onto a telephone mouthpiece for privacy; the
Bell System tried to forbid it as a "foreign attachment." The D.C. Circuit's holding established that a
subscriber's right to use a device privately beneficial and not publicly detrimental could not be
forbidden by the network operator.

## The arc (the principle fought out repeatedly)

The same boundary recurs across the history of networked devices:

- **Hush-A-Phone v. United States (1956)** — a passive attachment; private benefit, no public detriment.
- **Carterfone (1968)** — an *active* coupling of a radptotelephone to the wireline network; the FCC
  extended the principle to electrical attachment, opening the network to any device that did not harm it.
  This is the direct lineage to the modular-jack era and the third-party-equipment market.
- **The Bell System divestiture (1982–84)** — the structural endpoint of an operator that had treated the
  network's shared character as a warrant for control over what attached to it.
- **Later platform-era disputes (e.g. Apple / DOJ)** — the same tension, re-expressed for app and device
  ecosystems: where does an operator's legitimate interest in the integrity of a shared system end, and
  where does it become control over participants' private arrangements.

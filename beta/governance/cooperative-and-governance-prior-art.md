## Overview

Two sibling documents make the case for the cooperative form and then build it. The philosophy layer argues
the form is *necessary* — that a publicly-traded securitized corporation cannot constitute peer standing
with its participants, so a platform brokering social relationships must adopt a non-extractive form from
inception (`../philosophy/peer-standing-and-the-cooperative-form.md`). The manifestation layer builds it —
the foundation-plus-cooperative structure, the phasing, the marks, the four-pillar *Social Union*
(`foundation-cooperative-and-sustainability.md`). Both lean on a body of external prior art, and the
philosophy doc explicitly flags that literature as not yet sourced.

This document supplies exactly that grounding, and it supplies the half the necessity argument cannot reach
on its own: the existence-proofs that the form is not only necessary but *possible*. A structural argument
can establish that only a cooperative can host peer standing; it cannot, by itself, show that a cooperative
platform can pay its people, out-margin a venture-funded incumbent, raise seed capital without selling
governance, or hold its own trademark neutrally. Those are empirical questions, and the answer to each is a
living institution. This register names them, and for each carries the WHY — what it proves for the Croft
cooperative — so the co-op decision can be re-derived from the prior art rather than taken on faith.

## The named movement: Platform Cooperativism

The load-bearing gap this document closes first: the necessity argument argues *for* a cooperative platform
and even flags its supporting literature as unsourced, yet the movement that literature belongs to has a
name, a founder, and a founding text, and none of them appeared in beta.

**Platform Cooperativism Consortium** (Trebor Scholz, The New School) is the research hub and movement for
user- and worker-owned platforms. Its founding text is Scholz's 2014 essay *Platform Cooperativism vs. the
Sharing Economy*, which named the pattern: apply cooperative ownership to the platform, so the people who
create the value hold it, against the "sharing economy" that extracts it. Why this is load-bearing rather
than a citation of convenience: it supplies the vocabulary and the scholarly home for the entire form Croft
is adopting, and it is the body of work actively contesting the one genuinely open problem the co-op
decision generates — whether a cooperative can raise sufficient capital without issuing a claim that
reinstalls the extractive edge (the capital-formation question carried in the philosophy doc and in T33).

## Existence-proofs: the form is possible, not only necessary

The necessity argument establishes the form is required. These establish it is buildable. Each answers a
specific "but can a cooperative actually...?" objection with a living institution, and the objection each
answers is the WHY.

- **Stocksy United** (a Colorado limited cooperative association) — the gold-standard platform cooperative:
  ex-Getty/iStock founders, multi-class membership (founder / staff / artist), profitable, competing on
  quality-density rather than scale. **Proves** multi-class membership and progressive-decentralization work
  in one profitable entity — it is the direct model for the *Social Union*'s multi-class design.
  `[verified.]`

- **The Drivers Cooperative** (NYC) — driver-owned ride-share where drivers keep roughly 85% and the
  cooperative takes roughly 15%. **Proves** a cooperative can out-margin a venture-funded monopoly precisely
  by paying its contributors more — the answer to "won't the VC-funded competitor always win on price?" is
  that the extractive competitor's margin *is* the opening. `[verified.]`

- **Resonate** — a music-streaming cooperative whose *stream-to-own* model converts micro-payments into
  ownership and governance over time. **Proves** a non-token internal economy can route value to ownership
  without a speculative instrument — the precedent for the cooperative's internal-credit economy (value
  accrues to members through use, with no tradable token to dump). `[verified.]`

- **Social.coop** — a member-owned, member-governed Mastodon instance funded by member dues through Open
  Collective. **Proves** the closest living relative to the *Social Union*: a social platform actually run
  as a dues-funded cooperative. Its differences are as instructive as its likeness — it is single-protocol
  and carries no progressive-decentralization sunset — so it is the nearest analogue and the clearest place
  to read the gaps the Croft design must fill. `[verified.]`

- **Mondragon** — a worker-owned cooperative federation operating at industrial scale. **Proves** the
  cooperative form scales past the artisanal into a durable federation — the institutional-scale existence
  proof behind the whole bet. `[verified 2026-06-22.]`

- **Green Bay Packers** — the only community-owned team among the major US professional sports leagues, and
  the model was closed to imitators by league rule afterward. **Proves** community ownership can run a major
  competitive institution indefinitely; the subsequent ban is itself the tell that the incumbents treat the
  form as a threat worth prohibiting. `[verified 2026-06-22.]`

- **The credit-union lineage** — Schulze-Delitzsch → Raiffeisen → Desjardins → Filene. **Proves** the
  member-owned financial institution as a centuries-durable institutional model, and it is the source of the
  motto the whole non-extractive posture inherits:

  > "Not for profit, not for charity, but for service."
  >
  > — the credit-union movement (widely attributed to the Filene-era US movement)
  > `[dialogue-sourced 2026-06-22; verify attribution before reliance.]`

  The motto is load-bearing because it names the exact third position the *Social Union* occupies: not a
  business maximizing return, not a charity depending on donation, but a member-serving utility funded by
  the members it serves. `[verified 2026-06-22.]`

## The rug-pull disarmed: unsellable ownership and a credible exit, already demonstrated

The proofs above answer "can a cooperative *operate*?" These answer the prior question the whole form rests
on: does removing the extraction motive — either by an ownership structure that legally cannot be sold, or
by a forkable, exportable exit that makes enclosure pointless — actually disarm the rug-pull in the
historical record? Each is a documented case, and the WHY is the specific Croft thesis it grounds. The same
three cases appear in `../activism/platform-extraction-and-captured-labor.md` as the honest counterweight
that the extractive lifecycle is *contingent, not intrinsic*; there they qualify the indictment, here they
carry the affirmative existence-proof register for the cooperative-foundation and credible-exit theses.

- **Signal — governance, not decentralization.** The Signal Foundation launched in February 2018 as a
  501(c)(3) nonprofit with a roughly $50 million contribution from WhatsApp co-founder Brian Acton, and the
  Foundation wholly owns Signal Messenger. Signal is *architecturally centralized* — it runs its own
  servers and has historically resisted federation and third-party clients — so its protection is not that
  the servers do not matter but that the entity controlling them legally cannot be sold or taken public.
  Signal's president, Meredith Whittaker, has stated the structural logic plainly: privacy is not safe
  under shareholder-driven corporations, so Signal is structured as a nonprofit with no equity and no board
  demanding profit growth. The consequence she draws is that no one can sell the entity off — not an
  acquirer, and not its own leadership:

  > "even if I decided to become evil."
  >
  > — Meredith Whittaker, Signal president, on why the nonprofit structure makes Signal unsellable
  > `[UNVERIFIED, confirm against primary source before publish]`

  Co-founder Moxie Marlinspike has written that Signal never took venture funding or sought investment,
  because putting profit first would be incompatible with a sustainable, user-first project. **Proves** the
  sharpest form of the cooperative-foundation thesis: it is the *ownership structure*, not
  decentralization, that removes the extraction motive — you can be centralized-and-safe if the entity
  cannot be captured. This is a different defense from the open-protocol camp's (decentralize so the
  servers do not matter), and the contrast is exactly the one Croft draws. The caveat travels with the
  proof: Signal is unusually structured but not *funding*-solved — its 2024 expenses (about $38 million)
  exceeded its revenue (about $29 million), and it leans on donations plus the depleting Acton endowment,
  which is precisely why Croft's answer is a member-funded dues economy rather than a single benefactor. The
  endowment's irony is itself on-thesis: Acton's wealth came from selling WhatsApp to Facebook for about
  $19 billion in 2014, so a successful extraction funded the anti-extraction model. `[documented history;
  the $50M / $38M / $29M / $19B figures are research-sourced — confirm against the Signal Foundation's own
  filings before external reliance.]`

- **Dreamwidth — a forkable codebase plus a working importer is a real exit.** Dreamwidth is an open-source
  fork of the LiveJournal codebase with a full importer that carries a user's entire history — posts,
  comments, tags, icons. When LiveJournal relocated its servers to Russia in December 2016 (and a 2017
  terms-of-service change brought large blogs under Russian content law), users circulated Dreamwidth's
  import instructions as a way to take the whole archive somewhere they actually owned. **Proves** the
  credible-exit thesis literally: because the code was forkable and an importer existed, the community had
  somewhere to go, and the platform could not turn extractive without simply losing the people. This is the
  historical demonstration of Croft's own-your-data-plus-forkable design — the exit thesis the beta corpus
  otherwise asserts without its cleanest evidence. It also sits inside a sharper pattern in the *recoveries*
  of shuttered community worlds: every community that got its world back did so through volunteer
  preservation plus either a forkable codebase (Dreamwidth) or an eventual act of grace by the rights-holder
  — never through a right the community held at the outset. Making that right structural and present at
  inception is exactly the gap the cooperative form and the compatibility badge's forkability promise
  close. `[documented history.]`

- **Archive of Our Own (AO3) / the Organization for Transformative Works — a burned community rebuilding on
  infrastructure it owns.** AO3 is the thesis-perfect case because it was designed *as a direct response to
  a rug-pull*, by the very community that got burned. In 2007 fan-fiction writers faced two threats at once:
  FanLib, a for-profit fanfiction archive widely read as outsiders trying to profit from fans' work, and
  LiveJournal's "Strikethrough" (the May 29, 2007 mass suspension and deletion of journals and communities
  without warning, which swept up legitimate fan and even abuse-survivor communities). The response was
  structural rather than reactive: the writer astolat proposed a fan-controlled archive, and the deletions
  drove home that it was not safe to rely on any platform subject to a single owner's whims. The result —
  the OTW and AO3 — is now a top-tier site: a nonprofit, non-commercial, ad-free-by-principle archive run
  on open-source software the OTW developed, sustained entirely by user donations, hosting tens of millions
  of works for more than eight million users, and a 2019 Hugo Award winner. **Proves** that a community
  rebuilds durably on infrastructure it owns after an erasure, governed by a nonprofit that cannot be sold
  and funded by its own users — Wikimedia's model demonstrated a second time in a different domain, and the
  exact move Croft is built to enable. `[documented history.]`

## Governance DNA: how a commons stays governed at scale

The existence-proofs show the form runs; these show it can be *governed* without either collapsing into
tyranny-of-the-active-few or paralyzing under unfiltered consensus. This is where the co-op's governance
design draws its patterns.

- **The Rochdale Principles** (the Rochdale Society of Equitable Pioneers, 1844) — the founding governance
  code of the modern cooperative movement and still the backbone of the International Co-operative
  Alliance's statement of principles. **Proves** the member-governance discipline the whole form descends
  from, and one principle is directly load-bearing for a platform brokering relationships: *Education of the
  Members* — a cooperative cannot survive with an uninformed membership, because member-governance is only
  real if members understand what they are governing. This is the 1844 root beneath both the credit-union
  lineage and Platform Cooperativism, and the reason member education reads here as an operating obligation
  rather than an optional nicety. `[established history; Rochdale Pioneers, 1844.]`

- **Elinor Ostrom's commons work** — the empirical demonstration that communities sustain shared resources
  for centuries (the Törbel alpine commons, the Valencia *huertas*, the Bali Subak irrigation, the Maine
  lobster grounds), governed polycentrically rather than by a single central authority. **Proves** the scale
  answer for a member-governed platform: polycentric governance plus subsidiarity — decisions made at the
  smallest competent level, nested — is how a commons stays coherent as it grows, rather than requiring a
  single global governing body. This is the governance DNA the whole federation-plus-cooperative shape
  inherits. `[established scholarship; Ostrom, Governing the Commons, 1990.]`

- **Liquid Feedback / liquid democracy** — instantly-revocable, per-topic vote delegation (built by the
  German Pirate Party; a comparable internal system ran at Google as Google Votes). **Proves** "cheap exit
  at scale" — a member can delegate their vote on a topic and revoke it the instant the delegate diverges,
  which lets a large membership govern without everyone voting on everything. The reasoning travels with its
  own failure mode: the documented real-world failure is delegation *concentration* — a few super-delegates
  accreting outsized standing — and the antidotes are equally documented (delegation decay, per-delegate
  caps, bounded delegation chains, expiry, and full visibility of who holds whose vote). Carrying the
  failure mode is the point: the mechanism is adopted with its known antidotes, not as a naive good.
  `[dialogue-sourced 2026-06-20; verify before reliance.]`

- **Commons-DAO research** (De Filippi, Rozas, et al., *DAO design for the commons*, Frontiers in
  Blockchain, 2023). **Proves** an Ostrom-grounded alternative to the code-is-law mainstream: it treats
  forking as legitimate pressure on the powerful and holds that "none is essential," against the
  auto-executed-legitimacy model Croft rejects. It is the bridge between Ostrom's commons scholarship and a
  digital-native governance design. `[dialogue-sourced; verified Frontiers DOI 10.3389/fbloc.2023.1287249.]`

## Seed capital without capture

The central open problem the co-op decision generates is edge-preserving capital formation: how to fund
infrastructure without issuing a claim that smuggles the asymmetric owner/user edge back in. These are the
prior-art instruments that grant financial rights without governance — the raw material for a solution, not
the solution itself.

- **The Purpose Foundation / steward-ownership** — a structure in which ownership is held in stewardship for
  the mission and cannot be sold for private gain; control and economic rights are deliberately separated.
  **Proves** that "you cannot sell it" can be a binding legal structure rather than a mere promise — the
  seed-capital counterpart to the manifestation doc's bankruptcy-remote steward. `[verified.]`

- **Revenue-Based Financing / the *Demand Dividend*** — capped-return investor models in which the investor
  holds a financial right (a share of revenue up to a fixed multiple) with no governance right, and the
  stake *vanishes* once the cap is reached. **Proves** the precise shape a non-capturing seed instrument can
  take: financial participation that terminates, never converts to control, and never creates a permanent
  constituency with standing to enforce a profit-maximizing pursuit. This is the "capped revenue-share for
  seed capital" pillar of the *Social Union*, grounded in a real financing pattern. Documented instances of
  the capped-return structure in practice — Haferkater and Start.coop among them — matter to the WHY: the
  pillar rests on an instrument that mission-driven ventures have actually raised on, not a theoretical
  shape. `[verified.]`

## The fiscal-sponsor analysis: the concrete Phase-1 vehicle

This is the most operationally concrete section, and it maps directly onto the entity-phasing in the
manifestation doc. The question each vehicle answers: who holds the marks and takes the money before Croft
has its own entities, and who holds them permanently once it does. All three rows are dialogue-sourced and
must be confirmed against the organizations' own current terms before reliance.

- **Software in the Public Interest (SPI)** — the trademark-stewardship proof-of-concept for Croft's exact
  plan. SPI *owns* the Debian trademark (a registered US mark), the mark is *managed by* the Debian project,
  and SPI has licensed it to outside organizations; it holds other projects' marks as well (ArduPilot's among
  them). **Proves** the
  asset-holding-shell / community-runs-it model — a neutral body owning the mark while the project operates
  under it — which is exactly Croft's foundation-holds-the-marks / cooperative-operates-under-license
  intent. This is the best long-term asset-holding match and the single clearest existence proof that the
  two-body mark structure works. `[dialogue-sourced 2026-06-23, verify before reliance.]`

- **Software Freedom Conservancy (SFC)** — a permanent, neutral mark-holder. A 501(c)(3) that can hold
  copyrights, trademarks, and domains and provides trademark registration, policy, licensing, and
  enforcement (word-for-word the foundation's plan), backed by real GPL-enforcement legal muscle. It applies
  a strict OSI/DFSG bar and a slow rolling review and is explicitly "not a service provider." **Proves** the
  permanent-home option: the Phase-2-and-beyond destination if Croft matures into a major open-source
  effort. `[dialogue-sourced 2026-06-23, verify before reliance.]`

- **Aspiration** — the recommended *interim* foundation for Phase 1. A 501(c)(3) fiscal sponsor operating a
  short-term (roughly one-to-two-year) grantor/grantee model in which the project keeps its own board and
  its own IP — friendly to holding your own marks and graduating to your own 501(c)(3). It sponsors
  mission-aligned technology, data, and digital-rights projects with a focus on open and equitable
  governance and IP, which is a near-exact fit. **Proves** there is a live vehicle for the "take money and
  hold the mark before you incorporate" phase without surrendering control. The open item that must be
  verified: whether Aspiration will sponsor a *cooperatively-operated* project specifically — a charitable
  sponsor cannot house a member-owned business, so this is the seam to confirm. `[dialogue-sourced
  2026-06-23, verify before reliance.]`

A fourth vehicle sits in the same 501(c)(3) tech-sponsor class and is carried so the landscape is complete:
**Code for Science & Society (CS&S)** fiscally sponsors public-interest technology and open-science
projects, and belongs to the same nonprofit-half register as SPI and SFC — the option to weigh for the
grant-funded open-source-code side of a cooperative-plus-charitable tandem, distinct from the mark-holding
and interim-foundation roles the three phased vehicles above carry. `[dialogue-sourced, verify before
reliance.]`

One correction to a commonly-assumed vehicle, carried so it is not reached for by mistake: the Open
Collective *Foundation* dissolved at the end of 2024; the Open Source Collective is a separate 501(c)(6),
not a charitable 501(c)(3). Any reliance on "Open Collective" as a fiscal sponsor must specify which entity
and confirm its current status. `[verified.]`

## The anti-pattern failure lineage

Prior art includes what failed. Each of these is a documented death, and the cooperative model's pillars are
designed as direct answers — so this lineage is not a graveyard tour, it is the specification's negative
half. The WHY is the failure each embodies and the design choice it forces.

- **Ello** — took venture capital for an explicitly anti-advertising mission with no revenue engine, then
  went quiet and wiped user data. **Answers** why the model needs a real revenue engine and a *capped* exit
  rather than a VC veto: the mission cannot outlive the capital structure that funds it.

- **Ampled** — a musician-support cooperative run on uncompensated volunteer hours; it could not sustain its
  own maintenance and shut down in 2023 citing terminal burnout. **Answers** the single biggest long-term
  risk directly: pay the keepers. Reliable infrastructure requires reliable compensation, which is why
  market-rate cash for core builders is a first-class operating expense in the *Social Union*, not a
  patronage afterthought.

- **Steemit** — tokenized every interaction into a speculative, bot-farmed economy and was ultimately taken
  over in a hostile acquisition. **Answers** why the internal economy is closed and non-speculative: value
  in internal utility credits with no tradable token to dump, so there is no bot incentive and no hostile
  accumulation vector.

- **Diaspora** — an early decentralized social network undone in part by architectural naivety. **Answers**
  why the design must be sound at the substrate rather than merely well-intentioned; good governance cannot
  rescue a broken architecture.

- **Coomappa** — the sharpest line in the lineage: a project that "owned a cooperative but actually owned a
  technological dependency," captured by the white-label platform it was built on. **Answers** why Croft
  must own its client on open standards rather than rent its existence from a vendor — cooperative ownership
  of the *entity* is worthless if the *technology* is someone else's to revoke.

## What this establishes (and does not)

**Establishes.** That the cooperative form is not only necessary (the philosophy layer's structural
argument) but *possible* (a body of living institutions). It supplies the load-bearing name the beta corpus
was missing — Platform Cooperativism and Scholz's 2014 founding essay — and the existence-proofs that answer
the empirical objections the necessity argument cannot reach on its own: that a cooperative platform can be
profitable and multi-class (Stocksy), out-margin a venture monopoly by paying contributors (The Drivers
Cooperative), route value to ownership without a token (Resonate), run as a dues-funded social platform
(Social.coop), scale into a federation (Mondragon), and endure as a community-owned institution (Green Bay
Packers, the credit-union lineage). It grounds the two theses the whole form rests on in the historical
record: that an ownership structure which legally cannot be sold disarms the rug-pull (Signal's
governance-not-decentralization nonprofit; AO3's community-owned, unsellable archive) and that a forkable,
exportable exit does the same (Dreamwidth's forked codebase and importer; AO3 again, rebuilt after
deletion by the community that was burned). It grounds governance-at-scale in the Rochdale Principles' member-education
discipline, in Ostrom's polycentric commons work, and in liquid democracy carried with its own failure mode
and antidotes; it grounds non-capturing seed capital in steward-ownership and capped-return *Demand Dividend*
models used in practice; it names the concrete fiscal-sponsor vehicles for each phase (SPI as the
trademark-holding proof-of-concept, SFC as the permanent home, Aspiration as the interim foundation, with
Code for Science & Society as a fourth same-class option); and it registers the failure lineage (Ello, Ampled, Steemit,
Diaspora, Coomappa) as the negative specification each cooperative pillar answers.

**Does not establish.** It does not re-argue *why* the cooperative form is required — that is the philosophy
layer. It does not re-build the foundation-plus-cooperative structure or its phasing — that is the
manifestation doc. It does not certify the dialogue-sourced rows (the Liquid Feedback, credit-union motto,
and SPI/SFC/Aspiration rows carry verification flags and need a refresh pass against primary sources before
external use). And it does not resolve the cooperative legal-review gate (the entity statute, tax, and
foundation structure), which remains the user's decision under counsel, nor the edge-preserving
capital-formation problem it couples with — the defining open work this layer inherits.

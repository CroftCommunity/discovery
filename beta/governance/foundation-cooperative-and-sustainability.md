## Overview

The philosophy layer establishes *why* a cooperative form is the only one that can host peer standing
without reinstalling the extractive owner/user edge (see `../philosophy/peer-standing-and-the-cooperative-form.md`).
This layer does the other half: given that conclusion, how does the whole thing ground in the world as a
running, sustainable entity that pays its keepers, cannot be captured from inside or fenced off from
outside, and outlives its own operator by design.

The answer is a two-body structure:

- a **foundation** that stewards the neutral stack (the protocol, the reference implementation, and the
  product flavor) and holds the marks so no operator can hoard them; and

- a **cooperative** as the operating body, the legal and financial actualization of a form that does not
  reinstate the asymmetric edge.

Governance is not the argument for the form; it is the form built. Where this doc references the ethical
or epistemic basis (non-extraction as a value, "no right to remove the rights of others"), it inherits
that basis from philosophy and does not re-derive it.

## The two-body structure and its three decoupled layers

The core move is to hold three things with opposite tools for opposite goals, so that capture is a
*structural impossibility* rather than a promise anyone has to be trusted to keep.

```
   LAYER        TOOL                       RULE                       HELD BY
   Code         AGPL-3.0-or-later + DCO    maximally OPEN, forkable    nobody (the license governs;
   (commons)                               (gives away code control)   the foundation holds copyright)

   Brand        trademark, compat badge    maximally CONTROLLED        the neutral FOUNDATION
   (trust)                                 (only conformant carries     (licensed out)
                                            the name or badge)

   Operation    a cooperative (one of      runs the canonical          the COOP (free but
   (model)      potentially many)          instance under license       conditioned mark license)
```

Code and brand are opposite tools and must never touch. The AGPL says: take it, run it, change it, fork
it. The mark says: but you cannot call your fork by our name unless it conforms. The fork is free; the
*confusion* is what the mark prevents. Framed honestly, the mark is anti-counterfeiting for a community
asset, defensive of users' trust rather than mercantile, and it matters most exactly when success makes
the project a target for scammers wearing its face.

*Verification:* design intent (the user's own design thinking), not a legal filing.

### AGPL-3.0-or-later plus DCO: the structural-openness lock

Contributions come in under a **DCO** (Developer Certificate of Origin), *not* a CLA. A CLA would collect
relicensing rights in one party, which is a capture vector; a DCO grants none. So no party (not a future
founder, not a captured board, not an acquirer) can relicense or close the source. The inability to
relicense is the feature: a credible commitment in the game-theory sense, where you remove your own
ability to defect, which is what makes the promise trustworthy downstream. The "-or-later" choice defends
the network-use copyleft principle against future loopholes, at the cost of trusting the steward of the
license family with forward power; the sources lean "-or-later." A quiet benefit: because the code stays
open in everyone's hands regardless, the foundation ends up holding the brand and the copyright, not a
code monopoly.

*Verification:* design intent.

### Two tiers of mark: the house mark and the compatibility badge

Tier 1 is the **house mark** (Croft), held by the foundation and licensed tightly to the canonical
cooperative-run instance; a fork must rename. Tier 2 is a **compatibility badge** for the published
protocol ("Speaks Drystone"), the honest place for forks: a fork cannot use the house mark but can fly
the badge if it implements the protocol. The specification itself is the unowned commons (open spec,
controlled badge). The badge **starts as an honor system, not a formal certification mark** (a USPTO
certification mark carries heavyweight obligations: control the standard, do not use it on your own
goods, do not discriminate among qualifiers), with transparency as the early enforcement and a
revocation clause. One question is surfaced and not resolved: does flying the badge assert only
*technical* interoperability, or also adherence to the non-negotiable principles (that is, is the badge
where the principles get their teeth). That sits at the seam with the protocol specification and is
carried, not settled.

*Verification:* design intent.

### Entity phasing: do not build a governor before the thing it governs exists

Both bodies defer overhead through phasing, so that scarce early energy is not spent standing up
governance scaffolding for an idea that will change shape:

- **Phase 0 (now).** No legal entities. Code is AGPL-3.0-or-later; operate as an individual or informal
  group. Do the near-free brand separation immediately: a `TRADEMARK.md` stating the name and logo are
  NOT covered by the AGPL and forks must rename; use the name with a trademark symbol so common-law
  rights accrue from use; get the logo copyright assigned in writing by the artist on day one (the trap
  a well-known distribution fell into when the artist kept copyright for years).

- **Phase 1 (need to take money or hold the mark).** A charitable fiscal sponsor rents the *foundation*
  function, not the cooperative; a charitable sponsor cannot house a member-owned business. The
  cooperative is a member-owned business entity and is not what a charitable sponsor houses.

- **Phase 2 (the model is real and others want to replicate it).** Spin up the foundation to hold the
  marks neutrally and form the first cooperative as the reference operator. The gated, verified `.coop`
  TLD becomes available to the cooperative here and is itself a trust signal.

The marks move by **assignment with the goodwill** (an assignment "in gross" can invalidate the mark);
logo copyright moves by a separate written assignment; the code mostly stays put. The foundation
licenses the house mark to the cooperative **royalty-free but with quality-control conditions** (free
does not mean uncontrolled; active control is what preserves the mark), and the *same terms are
repeatable for future cooperatives*. Even footing is thereby made mechanical: no cooperative owns the
name, and the first has no structural privilege over the second. This is precisely why the foundation
must be named independently of the flagship: its neutrality collapses the moment it stewards a non-Croft
asset under a Croft-derived name. The foundation-name candidate (Noria) is carried below as pending, not
decided.

*Verification:* design intent.

## Non-extraction is anti-fragile: the defensive case

There is an old pattern: communities build real value through network effects, and that value is
repeatedly extracted, enclosed, or simply erased (whole platforms deleting decades of collective memory
because users never held custody). Enshittification names the *behavior*; the root cause is a
*capital-structure* one. Growth capital requires an exit, an exit requires an extraction story, and
lock-in supplies the leverage. Open protocol, portable identity, and user-held data remove the leverage
for a rug-pull; they do not remove the motive. Only funding and governance committed at inception removes
the motive, because you cannot retrofit governance after taking growth capital. A better owner is not a
structural fix: a good king is still a king.

Non-extraction is anti-fragile, not merely ethical. The venture model is brittle: it must grow
exponentially or die, burning ad spend to replace users fleeing an extractive product. A member-owned
utility only needs growth-pacing, enough to cover costs, and a revenue dip is leaner spend, not a death
spiral. The structural reason a survivor stays a survivor is that it cannot be sold. Governance, not
decentralization, is the defense, because it removes the motive. There is also a peer-reviewed support
beneath the value: introducing a price does not simply add a transaction on top of a social relationship,
it *crowds out* the intrinsic motivation that made the relationship real (the overjustification effect;
the canonical evidence is the day-care study where a fine for late pickups *increased* lateness by
converting a moral obligation into a purchasable price). A fee-for-everything model is corrosive because
metering participation erodes the contribution that constitutes the community; the cooperative's
internal-credit, dues-and-donation economy is the deliberate inverse.

*Verification:* the enshittification diagnosis is documented; the anti-fragility and crowding-out claims
are settled values, the latter externally grounded.

### Pay the keepers

Reliable infrastructure requires reliable compensation. Burnout is a failure of governance. Market-rate
cash pay for core builders is a first-class operational expense, prioritized above patronage dividends
and investor returns, and paid in cash for labor rather than coerced by mission-guilt or equity-in-lieu.
Just as a city hires engineers to keep the water running, the cooperative pays engineers to keep the
social commons running. The all-volunteer model is the single biggest long-term risk, and it is the
explicit antidote to a verified death (a pure volunteer cooperative that closed citing terminal
burnout).

### Founder-speed early, democracy by sunset

Shipping a consumer product by unfiltered committee consensus paralyzes execution; pure founder control
entrenches. The resolution is progressive decentralization: directive founder authority in the build
phase that *auto-expires* at hardcoded, externally-ascertainable milestones into one-member-one-vote,
with the sunset locked so it cannot be clawed back. This resolves the Day-One-democracy paralysis without
founder entrenchment.

*Verification:* settled values.

## The four-pillar Social Union

The mechanism that makes non-extraction survivable is a cooperative, a "Social Union," built on four
pillars. The legal vehicle envisioned is a **limited cooperative association** (the reasoning points at
Missouri Chapter 351; the statute sections, fees, and forms are excluded here and gated to counsel):

1. **A legal vehicle supporting multi-class membership and indivisible reserves.** In one entity: a
   founder or developer class with an early code veto; a patron or user class with one-member-one-vote,
   credit-union style; a nonpatron investor class with a capped return and limited or no vote. Governance
   attaches to the person; financial rights stack separately, so a person can hold two classes.

2. **Progressive decentralization with a hardcoded, externally-ascertainable founder sunset.** Milestones
   written as facts ascertainable outside the bylaws auto-strip founder power across phases, with a
   supermajority lock so the sunset cannot be altered by a small margin.

3. **A closed, non-speculative internal economy.** Member dues at cost; a capped revenue-share or royalty
   for seed capital (the revenue-based-financing or demand-dividend shape, terminating at a multiple cap);
   and tax-deductible donations routed through a parallel charitable entity. Value is locked in internal
   utility credits (they offset dues; there is no token to dump, so there is no bot incentive, which is
   the anti-Steemit choice) plus an indivisible reserve that serves as the cooperative endowment. In the
   tandem, the nonprofit owns the open-source code and indexers (grant-funded) while the cooperative owns
   the app, nodes, and member ledger (dues-funded).

4. **Labor as a first-class moral good.** Bylaws prioritize market-rate cash salaries for core builders
   above any patronage or investor payout, with a salary runway before dividends. Pay labor in cash
   (avoiding wage-law pitfalls and mission-guilt coercion); reserve patronage credits for participation.

The biggest honest risk is operational over-extension (the complexity tax of two entities plus
grant-tied salary precarity). The recorded resolution is a Minimal Viable Cooperative first: one revenue
engine, one entity, for the first stretch, with a path that lets a solo founder build the working model
and then capitalize the cooperative with the finished product at incorporation.

### The failure lineage of prior cooperative platforms

Each pillar answers a real, documented death rather than a hypothetical:

- **VC capture:** a platform that took venture money for an anti-VC mission with no revenue engine,
  shut down silently, and wiped user data. Answered by a capped exit, not a VC veto.

- **Volunteer burnout:** a pure cooperative on uncompensated hours that could not fix its own payment
  bugs and closed citing terminal burnout. Answered by paid builders.

- **Hyper-financialization:** a platform that tokenized every click into a bot-farmed casino and was
  taken over in a hostile buyout. Answered by internal credits, not a speculative token.

- **Architectural naivete:** a platform that leaked metadata to cloud middleware. Answered by native
  indexers.

- **White-label dependency:** the sharpest line, a project that "owned a cooperative but actually owned
  a technological dependency." Answered by an owned client on open standards.

And the default extractive arrangement is illustrated cleanly by a chat platform where volunteer
moderator labor is embedded in the number bankers are pricing while the moderators sit external to the
cap table (a tenant who can sublet, not an owner). Contribution and ownership are fully decoupled in that
default; the member-owned cooperative is designed to invert exactly that.

*Verification:* the failure lineage is verified as a failure pattern; the specific financial *figures*
for the private chat platform are [UNVERIFIED] (private company), while the labor-extraction reasoning is
the load-bearing part.

## The bankruptcy-remote steward and the pre-funded archive runway

The anti-rug-pull guarantee is deliberately *not* "we keep your data forever" (an unfundable
forever-liability). It is: if the operator dies, a pre-funded runway lets users retrieve and relocate
their data and community before it is gone. That is a finite, pre-fundable, bankruptcy-remote obligation,
and it is the more honest promise.

The structural core is a **bankruptcy-remote sibling entity or purpose trust** whose sole charter is
survivability, funded by a restricted reserve the operating entity cannot touch. The operating
nonprofit's plan of dissolution names this trust as recipient, and nonprofit-dissolution law forbids a
fire-sale to members. The steward is a fiduciary to the *data producers* (the users), not to a business
licensee, which is the novelty over standard software or data escrow (escrow protects a paying
enterprise licensee, never the individual end users).

The technical half makes the promise cheap. The cheapest durable storage pattern and the most survivable
one are the same thing: as little running as possible. A static, client-side-encrypted archive (one
bundled encrypted blob per user) on warm object storage with zero egress, fronted by a light auth gate
while the operator is alive. On operator death the auth layer *vanishes* and security still holds,
because encryption was always doing the real work; that degradation from "auth plus encryption" to
"encryption alone" is the key to survivability, since auth needs a running service and encryption does
not. Because the system is local-first (users already hold their own data), the rug-pull is partially
defanged before any legal structure engages. The survivability fund need only keep a static bucket
readable for the window, which is the difference between an impossible promise (permanence) and a
trivial one (a finite, pre-fundable graceful exit).

Honest caveats are carried: a plaintext identity-to-object index is a membership metadata leak (encrypt
it or derive keys from user secrets); password loss is unrecoverable by design; "infeasible to brute
force" is a moving target over decades and is honest only for a bounded window.

*Verification:* our design, evolving; a dedicated costed legal-options brief (purpose trusts, restricted
endowments, escrow-with-user-release trigger, dissolution-plan mechanics) remains outstanding.

## The non-mimicry moat: the affirmative competitive case

Anti-fragility is the *defensive* case for non-extraction (it explains why the cooperative survives what
kills the extractive competitor). The **non-mimicry moat** is the *affirmative* one, and it is distinct:
a cooperative capital structure makes a whole class of features *possible* that an extractive competitor
cannot copy without unwinding its own business model. Not a better version of what competitors do, but
the set of things they are structurally unable to do:

- protocol-level ad-blocking;

- transparency-as-a-feature (an open-book financial dashboard);

- a member-voted micro-grant treasury (community capital allocation);

- non-algorithmic, human-choice feeds;

- maintenance-first budgeting (a majority of spend to tech-debt and security rather than growth);

- user-controlled data TTL (the "digital living room," which also *lowers* hosting cost);

- a community-jury moderation model;

- digital-legacy and estate trusts;

- a "right-to-repair" forkability promise.

None of these is a feature an ad-funded, growth-capitalized platform can mimic, because each one removes
a lever that model depends on. The unifying, load-bearing claim is that non-extraction is a *feature
generator*, not only a cost; the individual items are product-roadmap-grade and are cited here for the
claim, not as commitments. The right-to-repair forkability promise carries one honest boundary: "experience
as a moat" is legitimate only against *extractive* competitors, never against *aligned forks*; real exit
(portable identity and data) must keep the fork path open, or the structure becomes the lock-in it
opposes.

*Verification:* settled value (the affirmative case); individual items are forward product notes.

## What this establishes (and does not)

**Establishes.** Non-extraction has concrete carriers here, not a slogan. A two-body structure (a
foundation stewarding the neutral stack, a cooperative operating it) whose three decoupled layers use
opposite tools for opposite goals; an AGPL-3.0-or-later plus DCO lock that makes closing the source
structurally impossible; a two-tier mark model (a foundation-held house mark, a compatibility badge)
that lets forks exist without impersonating; a four-pillar Social Union that pays its keepers and sunsets
founder power on schedule, each pillar answering a documented death in the failure lineage; a
bankruptcy-remote steward with a pre-funded archive runway that turns an impossible permanence promise
into a fundable graceful exit; and the non-mimicry moat, the affirmative case that non-extraction unlocks
a feature class an extractive competitor cannot ship without unwinding its own model. All of it defers
entity overhead through phasing while making capture structurally impossible from inside.

**Does not establish.** It does not re-argue *why* the cooperative form is required (that is the
philosophy layer). It does not resolve the cooperative and foundation legal-review gate, does not clear
the Noria foundation name, and does not solve edge-preserving capital formation (T33), which is the
defining open problem this layer inherits and the central constraint on any financing design proposed
above. All legal and financial specifics remain gated to counsel.

# Peer Standing, the Securitized Corporation, and the Cooperative Form

A structural argument that publicly-traded securitized corporations cannot constitute peer
relationships with their participants, and that brokering social relationships therefore
requires a cooperative form from inception.

author: assembled from conversation

status-legend:

[settled] = uncontested background, statable unmarked

[cited] = grounded in a retrieved primary or near-primary source, verified this session

[premise] = a structural/definitional claim being advanced, not a sourced finding

[ours] = reasoning or framing built in this conversation, labeled as such, not as literature

[tension] = an unresolved or contested point, surfaced not papered over

---

## 0. The claim being defended

The publicly-traded securitized corporation cannot constitute a peer relationship of equal
standing with its participants, because the form positions them outside the capital
constituency it is structurally bound to serve and grants them no reciprocal, enforceable
standing within it.

This is determinative, not moral-in-standing: it is a statement about what the form can and
cannot represent, like saying a client-server architecture cannot constitute role symmetry.
It produces moral consequences in effect, but the argument's force is structural, not an
indictment of intent.

The corollary, for building: a platform whose purpose is to broker peer relationships must be
constituted, from inception, in a form that never installs the extractive edge. The
cooperative is the form that does this by collapsing owner and user into a single standing.

## 1. What "peer" means

[cited] The English word descends from Latin *par*, "equal," via Old French *per* (Oxford
etymology). Its oldest English use is the peerage: peers were "equals in rank to the sovereign
in deliberative assemblies," holding the right to be judged only by other peers. The
Polish-Lithuanian *paritas* names the same structure: equal right to sit and vote within a
bounded body.

[ours] So even at the root, "peer" means equal *rights within a defined boundary*, never
equality across all axes. The peerage was equal among peers and superior to commoners. The
equality was always relative to a defined set. This is why the edge must be specified: peerhood
is a property of a relationship across a boundary, not a property of a whole system.

[cited] In networking, "peer-to-peer" was defined by *role symmetry* against client-server:
conventional peers are "equally privileged, equipotent participants," both supplying and
consuming, where a client only requests and a server only supplies (US Patent 9,406,043
background; the strict role test also appears in the IETF's RFC 5694, which excludes DNS from
P2P because resolvers only request).

[cited] "Equipotent" = Latin *aequus* (equal) + *potens* (powerful), "equal in power" (first
English use 1870-75). In set theory it means two sets in bijection: equal in standing,
*not* identical in content. Collins illustrates the word against the presence of a privileged
figure: "a group of equipotent peers versus a group that has one figure more powerful."

[ours] The drift in common usage fused three independent axes that the original definition kept
separate: role symmetry (what each node does), decentralization (where control sits), and mesh
topology (the connection shape). A system can be a flat mesh of unequal nodes or a centrally
coordinated set of equal ones. The "mesh expectation" reading is the drift; the role-symmetry
reading is the through-line.

[ours] The stronger definition, which survives contact with reality where "no clients or
servers" does not: *every participant holds equal right to serve and to request; no node holds
a right another is denied.* This defines by symmetric right, not by absence of roles, so a node
acting as a pure consumer at a given moment does not falsify it. Equality is equal *standing*,
not identical behavior.

## 2. Equality as standing, not sameness

[ours / premise] Two distinct things cannot be symmetric across every axis or they would be the
same thing. So "equal" in a peer relationship cannot mean identical throughput, uptime, or
behavior. It must mean equal standing: no participant holds a capacity over another that is not
reciprocal.

[cited] This is the central move of relational egalitarianism. Anderson (1999, "What Is the
Point of Equality?") reframes equality as "a kind of social relation between persons, an
equality of authority, status, or standing," against equality as a distribution of goods. To
stand as an equal means "one is entitled to participate, that others recognize an obligation to
listen and respond, that no one need bow and scrape." Equal standing "is more demanding and has
more substance than treating others the same."

[cited] The structure of the concept (equality = symmetric standing, not identical attributes)
is already borrowed into ML-fairness work when distributive metrics fail to capture power
asymmetries (arXiv 2024, citing Anderson 1999, Elford 2017, Schemmel 2012).

[ours] We borrow the *shape of the concept*, not the political conclusions. The philosophers
were not talking about protocols; the claim is that the concept has the same shape.

## 3. Domination as capacity, not exercise

[cited] Philip Pettit (b. 1945, Ballygar, Ireland; L.S. Rockefeller Professor at Princeton and
Distinguished Professor at ANU; revived the republican tradition, redefining freedom as
*non-domination* rather than non-interference) locates unfreedom in capacity, not conduct.

[cited] His definition (Pettit, "Freedom as Antipower," *Ethics* 106:3, 1996, p. 578):
"Someone has such power over another ... to the extent that (1) they have the capacity to
interfere (2) with impunity and at will (3) in certain choices that the other is in a position
to make."

[cited] The signature case (same essay, p. 581): a slave with a "generally beneficent master
who never interferes ... but nonetheless possesses the power to do so at any moment" is still
unfree, because "even assuming the absence of interference, the master can interfere with the
slave at will and with impunity" (Res Publica restatement). The wrong is the standing capacity,
not its exercise.

[cited] The popular gloss is the "eyeball test": non-domination is the ability to "look others
in the eye without reason for fear or deference." (Adopted as state policy by Zapatero's Spain,
2004-2010; documented in Pettit & Marti 2010.)

[cited] The AI-governance literature already extends this to structural capacity: "the test is
not whether the agent interferes wrongfully but whether it possesses the uncontrolled capacity
for arbitrary interference. A superintelligent agent whose capabilities radically exceed those
of its overseers possesses such capacity structurally."

[tension] Pettit's letter requires a *dominating agent*, "the dominating agent cannot be a
structure or a system." And Markell (in the *Journal of Political Power* debate) warns that
locating the wrong in *control* can confuse *domination* (one agent's arbitrary power over
another) with *usurpation* (occupying a position not rightfully one's own, a structural wrong).
These can come apart. So treating a *structural position* as the locus of domination is an
extension past Pettit's frame, not a quotation of it.

[cited] The bridge over that tension is Pettit's own later work. List & Pettit, *Group Agency*
(2011), argue group/corporate agents "really are ... over and above the individual agents who
compose them," and that group agency requires "a clear decision-making structure." A structured
group *is* an agent. So a structure-backed corporate position is itself an agent that can hold
the capacity to dominate; the structure does not bypass the agent requirement, it *constitutes*
the agent that meets it.

[ours] Reading the two works together, the "both" position falls out as Pettit's own combined
view: structure provides the means and constitutes the agent that expresses it. Where we diverge
from Pettit-the-author: he prescribes holding the corporate agent *accountable* (govern the
occupant); the center-free conclusion is to *eliminate the position* (vacate the seat). Same
ontology, opposite remedy. Put the invariant in the architecture, not in the conduct of whoever
occupies it, so it does not rely on anyone being "nice." The benevolent master proves nice
occupancy of a dominating seat is still domination.

## 4. Why coherent group agency tends to concentrate power

[cited] List & Pettit, *Group Agency* (2011, Ch. 2), prove: "there exists no aggregation
function satisfying universal domain, collective rationality, anonymity, and systematicity. Yet
any three of these four conditions can be satisfied together." (Founding paper: List & Pettit,
"Aggregating Sets of Judgments: An Impossibility Result," *Economics and Philosophy* 18:1, 2002;
the underlying paradox traces to Kornhauser & Sager 1986.)

The four conditions:

- Universal Domain: the rule accepts any combination of members' judgments.

- Collective Rationality: the group's output judgments are themselves consistent.

- Anonymity: all members count equally; no member's judgment is weighted more. (This is the
  equal-standing / no-privileged-node condition in formal dress.)

- Systematicity: each proposition is decided by the same rule, independent of the others.

[cited] Dropping anonymity yields a dictatorship or oligarchy; the surviving functions are
"oligarchies." Keep consistency and the structural conditions, and the math pushes toward a
privileged node.

[ours] The formal upshot for a center-free design: coherence and equal standing are in
mathematical tension at the group-agent level. A protocol that insists on anonymity (no
privileged peer) must relax one of the other three, most plausibly universal domain (constrain
what is decidable together) or systematicity (different decision rules for different question
types). The theorem tells you *which* knob to give on, and the answer is not the equal-standing
one.

## 5. The corporation as a value form

[cited] Delaware PBC statute (DGCL Subchapter XV). A PBC is "a for-profit corporation ...
intended to produce a public benefit" (§362(a)). Directors face a tri-partite balancing
requirement: manage "in a manner that balances the stockholders' pecuniary interests, the best
interests of those materially affected ..., and the ... public benefit[s]" (§365(a)). The word
is *balances*, not *subordinates*; "for-profit" is retained.

[cited] Three structural tells that the PBC *fences* but does not *abolish* the extractive
engine:

- §365(b): a director "does not have a duty to any third parties on account of such third
  party's interest in the public benefits." The benefit-class has no standing to enforce the
  benefit. It is a direction to the board, not a right held by anyone.

- §367: only stockholders owning at least 2% of shares (or, if listed, the lesser of 2% or
  $2,000,000 in market value) may sue to enforce the balance. The only enforcers are the capital
  constituency.

- §365(b) safe harbor: a balancing decision satisfies fiduciary duty if "informed and
  disinterested and not such that no person of ordinary, sound judgment would approve", the
  rational-basis floor, and charters may eliminate monetary liability for it entirely.

[cited] §366: the benefit report is biennial and goes *to stockholders*, not to the public or
the benefit-class.

[premise] The publicly-traded securitized form makes return-to-external-capital its
*structurally dominant pursuit*, because the share is a freely tradable residual claim held
purely as a pecuniary interest, continuously priced on a secondary market, and held by a
constituency detached from being user, worker, or community.

[cited] Delaware law confirms the structural dominance. In *eBay Domestic Holdings v. Newmark*,
16 A.3d 1 (Del. Ch. 2010), the controllers of craigslist openly pursued a community-service
mission over profit; Chancellor Chandler struck down their defensive rights plan: "Having chosen
a for-profit corporate form, the craigslist directors are bound by the fiduciary duties ... [which]
include acting to promote the value of the corporation for the benefit of its stockholders. The
'Inc.' after the company name has to mean at least that." "Directors of a for-profit Delaware
corporation cannot deploy a rights plan to defend a business strategy that openly eschews
stockholder wealth maximization."

[tension] Both anchor cases are routinely overread, and honesty about scope makes the argument
stronger, not weaker:

- *Dodge v. Ford* (Mich. 1919): the "primarily for the profit of the stockholders" line is
  *dicta* in a dividend dispute, not a purpose-holding. The case is "often misread or mistaught
  as setting a legal rule of shareholder wealth maximization," had near-zero legal influence
  before 1980 (37 citations 1919-79 vs. 1,108 in 1980-2019), and is Michigan, not Delaware. A
  weak anchor; useful only as intellectual history of how the norm crystallized.

- *eBay* is Delaware and modern, but bounded: the wealth-max requirement bit under *enhanced
  scrutiny* on a defensive measure (a poison pill). The controllers kept their culture via the
  upheld staggered board; they just could not use a pill to bind future/minority shareholders.
  Commentators split on how far it stands for "shareholder value is the only permissible
  objective."

[ours] The scope limits of *eBay* do not touch the refined claim, because the claim is about the
*structural pull*, not an absolute duty in every decision. And the litigation is *generated by*
the tradable security: the controllers' fear was the secondary-market transfer of the residual
claim to a monetizing acquirer. Strip the tradable security and there is no *eBay*. That is the
cleanest evidence that the extractive pull is a property of the *securitized claim*
specifically.

## 6. Why the cooperative is the compatible form

[premise] A peer relationship is by nature not driven by extraction as its dominant premise.
Securitization is. So a platform that represents and reinforces peer standing, funded through
the securitized form, has a funding structure working against its purpose.

[cited / ours] The cooperative form severs the three couplings that turn value-circulation into
structural domination. These follow from the ICA Statement on the Cooperative Identity (1995),
verified against the ICA primary this session; the [cited] marks what the Statement establishes,
the [ours] marks the structural reading.

- Member-held, non-tradable claim. The ICA Statement establishes member economic participation
  with members contributing equitably to and democratically controlling the capital, capital
  "usually owned in common," and "limited compensation, if any, on capital subscribed as a
  condition of membership." [ours] Non-transferable membership is the standard *legal
  implementation* of this in cooperative statutes rather than a line in the ICA Statement
  itself; the structural effect is that the claim cannot detach from use, so there is no
  secondary market in which it can be priced and maximized. (Directly contrasts the *eBay* fact
  pattern.)

- Patronage-based return. The Statement directs that members allocate surpluses partly toward
  "benefiting members in proportion to their transactions with the cooperative." [ours] So
  surplus returns in proportion to *use*, not capital stake, and owner-interest and
  user-interest become the same vector rather than competing ones. The member sits on both sides
  of the edge.

- One-member-one-vote. The Statement is explicit: "in primary cooperatives members have equal
  voting rights (one member, one vote)." [ours] Governance does not scale with capital, which is
  literally equal-standing-across-a-defined-edge encoded in the control structure, the
  peerage's *paritas* as a voting rule.

[premise] The cooperative does not *guarantee* alignment, and this is the point rather than a
weakness. A cooperative is only as aligned as its charter and governance make it; a badly
chartered co-op fails. What the form uniquely provides is that the alignment can be made a
*binding, enforceable charter commitment held by the constituency that uses the thing*, rather
than a discretionary indulgence the dominant pursuit may revoke. The securitized form cannot do
this: §365(b) and *eBay* mean the capital constituency's standing is structural and the
benefit-class's is, at most, a direction to the board. The cooperative is therefore
*necessary but not sufficient*: it is the precondition that makes alignment bindable, the
charter and governance are where alignment is actually bound, and (in this project) the protocol
layer is what makes the charter enforceable in operation rather than merely promised. The form
is the seat; the charter is who sits in it; nothing about the form fills the seat correctly on
its own.

[ours] So the cooperative does not deny that value circulates; it dissolves the *asymmetric
edge* across which extraction runs. Circulation-within-a-peer-set replaces
extraction-across-a-client-server-edge. "Abolish extraction" and "make it peer-symmetric" turn
out to be the same move described at two layers. It also removes the advertiser as the customer
entirely (see §7a): when the member is who the entity serves, the member's attention is not
inventory, because there is no third party it is being produced for.

[ours / qualified] "It must start that way to continue." The defensible version is not a logical
necessity but a path-dependence claim: conversion *into* the securitized form is easy and
incentivized; conversion *out* is near-impossible, because once tradable claims exist, they
create a constituency with legal standing to enforce the dominant pursuit (*eBay* is the proof ,
the moment a profit-seeking shareholder exists, directors cannot structurally defend the
non-pecuniary mission). The ratchet turns one way. You cannot reform back to peer standing from
inside, because the reform needs the consent of the constituency whose standing-advantage it
would remove.

[tension] The absolute "only the cooperative" overstates. Foundation-ownership (Novo Nordisk,
Bosch, Patagonia 2022), perpetual-purpose trusts, and steward-ownership structures protect
non-extractive purpose without being cooperatives. The surviving claim: the cooperative is the
form that *dissolves* the edge (rather than *fencing* it), and because the extractive edge is
legally entrenched-against-removal once installed, a peer-standing platform must adopt a
non-extractive form from inception.

[tension] The hardest real-world constraint: capital formation. A social-graph platform has
infrastructure costs and network-effect dynamics historically funded by exactly the securitized
venture capital the argument rules out. Notably, the ICA Statement anticipates this exact
pressure: cooperatives may raise external capital, but the autonomy principle requires they do
so "on terms that ensure democratic control by their members and maintain their cooperative
autonomy" (verified against the ICA primary this session). The open question, actively
contested in the platform-cooperativism literature, is whether a co-op can raise sufficient
capital without issuing claims (non-voting preferred, outside investor classes) that smuggle the
asymmetric edge back in. This is not a flaw in the logic; it is the engineering constraint the
logic generates, and the ICA's own autonomy condition is the principled statement of it: the
funding instrument must itself be edge-preserving.

## 7. The domain mismatch (the crux)

[ours] The securitized form is well-fitted to *goods and services* and structurally misfitted to
*the brokering of social relationships*. The difference rests on three conditions that hold for
goods and fail for relationships:

- Aligned: for a good, more use-value drives more extraction, so capital-dominance and
  customer-satisfaction point the same way. For brokered relations, the extractive pursuit
  (engagement, attention, data, reach) is frequently achieved by *degrading* relationship
  quality, so the interests compete.

- Separable: a good is handed across the counter and its quality is independent of the buyer's
  governance rights. In a social graph there is no separable good; the "product" is constituted
  by the participants' own standing toward each other, so the dominant pursuit acts on the very
  thing it claims to broker.

- Exitable: a goods transaction completes and releases you. A social graph holds your
  relationships hostage to continued participation; network effect is lock-in, so the asymmetric
  edge becomes permanent and ambient rather than transactional.

[ours] This is why one line is the strongest in the whole argument: a firm does not have to
degrade the chair to extract value from it, but a social platform extracts *by shaping the
relations themselves*. The chair company's incentive is to make a good chair. The platform's
incentive points at the relationship.

### The empirical limb, grounded

The empirical case is built in layers of decreasing certainty, and the argument leads with the
most certain. The point is that the thesis does **not** depend on the contested layers. The
strongest plank requires no claim that anyone's attitudes were changed; it requires only that the
optimizer targets something the user did not choose and that pulls against what the user says
they want. Every claim below carries the epistemic status that survived verification against its
primary; where a source is contested, the contest is stated rather than smoothed.

A framing note, [ours]: the strongest version of this argument does not rest on any single large
causal effect. It rests on (a) the platform's own internal admission that engagement and
endorsement diverge, (b) the mechanism that makes that divergence structural, (c) the reflexive
harm that makes it self-perpetuating, and (d) honest acknowledgment that population-level
*attitude* effects are small or contested. The disagreement in (d) is a feature of the case, not
a hole in it, because the attitude-change claim was never ours.

**Plank A: the misaligned objective, admitted by the company (the strongest, least contestable
plank).**

[cited] The cleanest evidence is the platform stating, in its own words, that engagement and
endorsement come apart. In Mark Zuckerberg's November 2018 public note "A Blueprint for Content
Governance and Enforcement," he wrote that as a piece of content nears the line of what is
allowed, "people will engage with it more on average, even when they tell us afterwards they
don't like the content." That is the company describing the wedge between revealed behavior and
reflective preference, and describing its own ranking's pull toward the former. (Status:
company-internal-admission; signed public note.)

[cited] Independent causal audit corroborates the same wedge from the outside. A pre-registered
algorithmic audit found Twitter's engagement-based ranking amplified emotionally charged,
out-group-hostile content that users themselves said made them feel worse about their political
out-group, and users did *not* prefer the political tweets the algorithm selected, so the
engagement algorithm underperformed at satisfying their stated preferences. The authors state the
result amplifies such content "beyond users' choices of who to follow, and even beyond what
users' stated preferences at the tweet-level would indicate" (Milli, Carroll, Wang, Pandey, Zhao
& Dragan, "Engagement, user satisfaction, and the amplification of divisive content on social
media," *PNAS Nexus* 4:3, March 2025, pgaf062; verified verbatim).

[tension] The same PNAS Nexus paper found the *stated-preference* alternative had its own failure
mode: ranking by stated preference reduced hostile content but produced "a potential
reinforcement of pro-attitudinal content," tending to keep users in a belief-confirming bubble.
So the paper does *not* support a clean "stated-preference good, engagement bad" contrast. This
sharpens the conclusion rather than weakening it (see §7a): *both* behavioral and
stated-preference optimization have failure modes, so the resolution is not "rank by stated
preference instead"; it is to remove the external customer so the optimization target stops
being attention at all.

[ours] **Scope of Plank A.** The Milli finding is specifically about
political/partisan/out-group-hostile content, on Twitter, against a reverse-chronological
baseline. It is strong causal evidence for *one instance* of relationship-shaping: emotional and
political amplification against stated preference. The extension to "engagement systems shape the
texture of relationships generally" is our reasoning, not the paper's finding, and is marked as
such wherever the argument leans on it.

**Plank B: the mechanism that makes the divergence structural.**

[cited] Platforms infer what users want from what they do, but, as Kleinberg, Mullainathan &
Raghavan put it, "we often make choices in the moment that are inconsistent with what we actually
want" ("The Challenge of Understanding What Users Want: Inconsistent Preferences and Engagement
Optimization," *Management Science* 70:9, 2024, pp. 6336-6355; verified). They model media
consumption under inconsistent preferences and show engagement optimization can amplify content
misaligned with users' own underlying values.

[tension] KMR locate the *deepest* problem not in corporate incentives but in the
revealed-preference assumption itself, which they say "transcends the specific incentives of any
particular platform." Their model uses an explicitly *altruistic* platform that "simply wants to
maximize user utility, but only observes engagement," and still finds it pulled toward amplifying
engagement absent better signal. So some shaping is endemic to behavioral optimization, not
unique to the extractive form.

[ours] Resolution: the revealed-preference trap is a *capability* problem (true utility is hard to
measure) that better signal can in principle improve. The securitized ad-funded form converts
that contingent trap into a *binding* one, because the structurally dominant pursuit gives the
entity a standing reason *not* to invest in closing the gap. So behavioral optimization makes
relationship-shaping *possible*; the securitized form makes it *structurally preferred* and binds
the entity *against* removing the bias.

**Plank C: the reflexive harm: the corrective faculty and the damaged faculty are the same
(the sharpest plank, now sourced).**

[cited] The natural counterbalance to a distorted social-information environment is collective
sense-making: people comparing notes, correcting each other, rebuilding shared reference. The
finding that matters is that this corrective faculty runs on exactly what the optimization
degrades. Milano, Mittelstadt, Wachter & Russell name the mechanism: algorithmic targeting
"isolates individual consumers, causing what we call epistemic fragmentation," which "inflicts
structural damage to the public forum" ("Epistemic fragmentation poses a threat to the governance
of online targeting," *Nature Machine Intelligence* 3, 466-472, 2021; verified verbatim). Their
central move is the reflexive one: the two natural correction strategies (raising individual
awareness; external monitoring) "fail because even sophisticated individual consumers are
vulnerable in isolation," and both failures are attributed to their failure to address epistemic
fragmentation. The corrective loop cannot form because the conditions for forming it are what is
being optimized away.

[ours] **The extension to ranking is our step, and it is short.** Milano et al. develop the claim
for targeted advertising and profiling, not engagement-ranking feeds. The structural mechanism is
the same (personalization isolates; isolation disables collective correction), and the extension
is supported rather than free-floating: a pre-registered collaborative-filtering experiment
(Burton, Herzog & Lorenz-Spreen, CogSci 2024 / CHI 2026, total N = 1,500) found "simple changes
to how posts are sampled and ranked can affect the beliefs people form," measured on both belief
accuracy and consensus, and framed curation harms as arising "when their objectives are
misaligned with user values and welfare." So the chain is: Milano sources the reflexive structure
for targeting; Burton sources that ranking specifically shapes belief and consensus; joining them
into the four-faculty claim (shared reference, good-faith disagreement, calibrating trust,
attention) is our structuring move, marked as such. *(Burton's constructive corollary is worth
carrying: the same experiment shows curation can be tuned for prosocial outcomes using only
natural engagement signals, so the ranking layer is not inherently corrosive; it is corrosive
under the engagement objective specifically. That is precisely the structural point: the problem
is the misaligned objective plus the removed governor, not ranking as such.)*

**Plank D: the honest other side, at full strength (this is what makes the rest credible).**

[cited] The strongest opposing evidence is the Meta / 2020-election collaboration. Switching
consenting users from the algorithmic feed to a reverse-chronological one sharply changed what
they saw and how much they engaged, yet "did not significantly alter levels of issue
polarization, affective polarization, political knowledge, or other key attitudes during the
3-month study period" (Guess et al., *Science* 381:398-404, 2023; verified). Companion null
results (Nyhan et al., *Nature* 620, 2023, on like-minded sources; Bail et al., *PNAS* 2018,
finding exposure to opposing views can *increase* conservative polarization) point the same way:
short-term feed changes do not reliably move stable attitudes.

[tension] Two things complicate the null, both confirmed against primaries. First, during the
experiment Meta introduced 63 "break-glass" emergency changes to the algorithmic feed, stated
in the published *Science* correspondence, not just secondary commentary, which critics argue
dampened the control condition. Second, a 2026 field experiment on X found the opposite
*direction*: switching users *onto* an algorithmic feed shifted opinion (toward more conservative
positions) and increased engagement, while switching off had no comparable effect (Guriev et al.
/ "The political effects of X's feed algorithm," *Nature*, Feb 2026). The asymmetry is itself the
finding: switching off does little, switching on does something.

[ours] **Why Plank D does not dent the thesis; it disciplines it.** The contested literature
mostly refutes the *overclaim* (the algorithm radicalizes the average user; feeds flip stable
attitudes), which was never our claim. Every one of these studies *does* find effects on exposure,
emotion, and engagement, the relational/expression layer the thesis targets, and disputes only
deep-attitude change. One of the lead researchers makes the calibration himself: "no one is
saying this means that social media has no negative effects ... but these are three interventions
that have been widely discussed and none of them measurably changed attitudes" (Nyhan, in
*Science* coverage, 2023). The defensible reading is the choice-architecture one: shaping
defaults, salience, and adjacency is influence over the *conditions* of choice even where it does
not override the choice, and that is a normative claim about what counts as autonomy, stated as
the philosophical claim it is, not as a settled empirical fact. A disadvantaged-but-living
corrective is a more believable and more damning claim than capture.

**A documented industry instance (attributed, not independently established).** Two on-record
items make the abstract concrete, both used as self-indictment rather than as established
causation:

[cited as reporting] Facebook's January 2018 shift to rank for "Meaningful Social Interactions"
(MSI). Internal documents released by Frances Haugen record Facebook data scientists writing the
approach had "unhealthy side effects on important slices of public content such as politics and
news," with "strong evidence" attributable to the algorithm; the Washington Post reported, from
an internal ~April 2019 Facebook report, that European political parties told Facebook they had
shifted toward negative messaging to chase engagement (present this as Facebook's own internal
report as reported, not as the parties' verbatim words). A peer-reviewed note corroborates the
US-side pattern but is correlational and hedged ("may have amplified"; Reuning, Whitesell &
Hannah, *Research & Politics* 9:2, 2022). Scope discipline: the Parker and Palihapitiya insider
quotes describe 2007-2011-era design intent, so they support "the loop was built deliberately,"
never "this is how Meta operates now."

[contested-litigation] The newest and most contested item is "Project Mercury": a November 2025
unredacted filing in the N.D. Cal. social-media MDL alleges Meta ran an internal
deactivation study (with Nielsen, ~2019-2020) finding that users who stopped Facebook for a week
"reported lower feelings of depression, anxiety, loneliness and social comparison," that a staff
researcher wrote internally "the Nielsen study does show causal impact on social comparison," and
that another worried non-publication would look like "tobacco companies ... knowing cigs were bad
and then keeping that info to themselves." This must be paired, every time, with Meta's on-record
rebuttal (spokesman Andy Stone): the study was a flawed, unblinded pilot whose finding reflects
expectation effects, people who already believed Facebook was bad for them feeling better when

### 7a. The advertising model: shaping is constitutive, not incidental

[ours / premise] Name the revenue model and the softest joint hardens. When extraction runs
through selling user attention to advertisers, the participant is not the customer; the
participant is the *good being sold*, and the advertiser is the customer. That single fact
re-seats everyone on the edge: the peer relationship the platform claims to broker is,
structurally, the *inventory*.

[ours] So the shaping is no longer a side effect of a flawed proxy that the platform might fix.
It is the product specification. The dominant pursuit is not "maximize engagement and
unfortunately that degrades relationships"; it is "produce attention to sell," and the
relationships are the substrate attention is farmed from. Shaping the field toward
attention-maximization is the form executing its function correctly, not failing.

[ours] This is why "no matter the veneer" is exact and not rhetorical. The veneer (connection,
community, meaningful interaction) is the *acquisition surface*, what brings the substrate in.
The function underneath is to render that substrate as sellable attention. A defender who says
"but we genuinely want connection" is not lying; connection is the means by which the inventory
is produced. From the form's point of view the two are not in tension, better connection that
produces more attention is *more product*. The tension appears only from the participant's
point of view, where their relationship is optimized for a purpose that is not theirs and that
they hold no standing within.

[ours] It also tightens the §7 resolution. The revealed-preference trap stops being a
measurement limitation the platform might close and becomes the *correct instrument*, because
attention is precisely revealed-by-behavior, and attention is the product. The platform has no
structural reason to close the gap between what you do and what you value, because what you *do*
is what it sells. Stated-preference alignment is not underbuilt by neglect; it is orthogonal to
the product. You cannot sell what someone *wishes* they had attended to. (This is exactly why the
PNAS Nexus [tension] above does not rescue the form: even though ranking-by-stated-preference has
its own bubble failure mode, the ad-funded form has no reason to choose it, because stated
preference is not what it sells.)

[ours] The epistemic shape is "amoral but not without intent." Amoral: the group agent has no
native moral standing (the aggregation / group-agency analysis, its will is detached from any
member's values by construction). Not without intent: it is not random or indifferent; it has a
definite, bounded objective, produce attention to sell. The intent is real but *narrow*, and
the narrowness is the point. It is not malevolent toward the relationships; it is *indifferent*
to them except as feedstock, and indifference-with-a-purpose is what shapes them.

[ours] Scope boundary, held honestly: "the user is the product" describes the *ad-funded* model
specifically, not all securitized platforms. Subscription, transaction-fee, and marketplace
models securitize without selling attention; the argument against those runs through the §5-§7
extraction-edge analysis, not this one. The attention-farming claim is the sharpest blade and
the most specific; it cuts cleanest exactly where the revenue model is advertising, and is
stronger as a precise claim about a named model than as a generalization.

### 7b. Stewardship is not in the objective function

[ours] A correction to a loose word, because it makes the claim stronger. It is tempting to say
attention-farming "depletes" the relational field, as monocropping depletes soil. But that
overclaims a *direction*. So does "it sustains." What the structure actually fixes is the
*objective*, and stewardship is not in it.

[settled] Stewardship is not entailed by extraction. It appears only under a contingent
condition: when the extractor cannot externalize or escape the depletion, *and* the asset's
recovery horizon is shorter than the horizon the extractor optimizes over. Remove either and
stewardship evaporates. The bison commons (no excludability, individually rational to take now,
herd collapsed), old-growth timber and collapsed fisheries (recovery horizon longer than
capital's realization horizon, so liquidate-now dominates) are the cases where stewardship
"obviously should" have applied and did not, because the intent did not require it. (Historical
illustrations, not sourced this turn; flag for primary citation if load-bearing.)

[ours] So the only thing assertable about the platform's relation to the relational field is
that *stewardship is not what is being optimized for.* Not "it depletes" and not "it sustains" ,
both name a direction the form does not fix. Whether the field persists or is exhausted is an
*unoptimized side variable*, floating free, determined by whether maximizing the crop this
period happens to coincide with preserving the ground. Sometimes it will. Nothing in the form
makes it. This is the benevolent-master structure once more: not "the master harms you" but
"your wellbeing is not the master's concern, and persists only at the master's convenience."

### 7c. Where the metaphor inverts, and why that is the point

[ours] The soil metaphor is exactly true *from the farmer's perspective, and only from there*,
and its breakdown is not a flaw but the indictment made visible.

[ours] In the farming picture the soil is passive substrate with no standing, no perspective, no
claim. The whole frame is the cultivator's: yield, depletion, stewardship-or-not are assessed
from the farmer's vantage, and the soil's "interests" are at most a prudential input to the
farmer's calculation. That is why the metaphor feels complete: it describes the platform's
logic precisely *because* it adopts the platform's perspective, in which the relational field
genuinely is just ground to be worked.

[ours] But the relational field is made of people, and people are peers, the one thing soil
definitionally is not. So calling relationships "soil" already takes the farmer's view, in which
participants have no standing of their own. The metaphor is accurate as a description of *how the
form sees us* and false as a description of *what we are*. That gap is the argument.

[ours] This closes the loop with where the chain began. A peer is, by definition, the one whose
perspective counts, equal standing means your vantage is not reducible to someone else's input
variable. Soil has no perspective that registers; that is what makes it soil. So the deepest
statement of the mismatch: the ad-funded securitized form can only apprehend the relational
field from the cultivator's perspective, in which participants are substrate, and that
apprehension is structurally incapable of registering them as peers, because registering a peer
means granting their perspective independent standing, and the form's objective function has no
term for it. The health and culture of the field "outside of what it yields" is not
under-weighted by the form; it is *invisible* to it. It does not show up as small. It does not
show up.

[ours] That is deeper than "stewardship is not optimized for," which still lives inside the
farmer's frame. The form cannot even *represent* the proposition that the field has its own life
worth caring about on its own terms, because to the form there is no "its own terms", only yield
and the conditions of yield. The quiet, the resolved, the sufficient, the things that matter to
people *as people and not as attention-sources* are not low-priority; they are not in the form's
ontology at all.

[ours] The corrected grip: *they can only farm us, and we are not a field.* The form can only
till, uproot, and disturb the soil of our relationships for attention; that is its entire
purpose, and we are not soil to be tilled. Or we can choose not to be. That choice is the
cooperative form, because only when the field and the cultivator are the same body is there no
external vantage from which your relationships are someone else's ground.

## 8. Conclusion

[ours] Net: a publicly-traded securitized corporation cannot *constitute* a peer relationship
with its participants, only treat them well at discretion, which is the benevolent master, the
opposite of equal standing. The incompatibility is determinative (about what the form can
represent), not moral-in-standing (about anyone's intent), even though it produces moral
consequences in effect. Any claim of peer-relationship from inside the form is structurally void
rather than necessarily insincere, because the participant holds no reciprocal, enforceable
standing, and no individual's values can change this, because the asymmetry is a property of
the form, not its occupants.

[ours] The form fits goods, where extraction is aligned, separable, and exitable. It misfits the
brokering of social relationships, where extraction competes with, is inseparable from, and locks
in the relation it claims to serve. Therefore the gap between a social platform's stated intent
(peer connection) and its structural ability (extraction) is not an execution failure that
better people could fix; it is a property of the form, and only a different form can close it.

[ours] This converts a critique into a spec requirement. "The form must be capable of
constituting peer standing" becomes a design constraint, the way "must tolerate f Byzantine
faults" is. The cooperative is the form that meets it, by dissolving the owner/user edge, but
meeting it is a *capacity the form provides, not a guarantee it supplies*: the alignment is bound
by charter and governance and (here) enforced at the protocol layer, and a cooperative whose
charter does not bind it will fail like anything else. Because the extractive edge is legally
entrenched once installed, the platform must be constituted that way from inception. The
unresolved engineering problem this generates, and the right next object of work, is
edge-preserving capital formation: funding the infrastructure without issuing any claim that
reinstalls the asymmetric edge, which is the ICA autonomy principle's own condition, stated as
an engineering constraint.

[ours] The deepest question underneath, the one the strongest line forces: a securitized platform
brokering social relations places a structurally non-moral agent, one whose dominant pursuit is,
by the group-agency analysis, detached from any member's values, in the position of shaping not
merely the *medium* of human communication but the *relationships themselves*. The argument here
is that this is a category placement we can decline by choosing the form, not a fate we inherit
by going online.

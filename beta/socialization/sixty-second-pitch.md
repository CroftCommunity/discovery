# Elevator pitch: the relational-field reframe

Three registers of one idea, leading with the reframe (which is what makes "try a different shape"
and "the full stack" land). Pick by setting. All three avoid the privacy frame on purpose, because
privacy has lost to convenience for twenty years; this is an authorship-and-agency claim instead.

---

## The one-liner

They don't just watch your relationships. They author them, and you are not the writer, and not
the one they're written for.

*(Alternate, soil-native version, for readers who'll get the companion's metaphor:)*
We are not the customer in their fields. We are the soil. And we are not soil.

---

## The spoken version (~20 seconds)

The worry about social media has always been privacy, that they can see you. But that's not the
deep harm, and it's never been enough to make anyone leave. The deep harm is that an
advertising-funded platform doesn't just carry your relationships, it *shapes* them, which
conversations rise, who you hear from, what starts to feel normal to say and feel, and it does
that to maximize an outcome that is not yours: your attention, sold to someone you'll never meet.
You're not the customer. Your relationships are the product. And the shaping has to stay invisible,
because a feed that admitted it was tuning you against your own stated preferences would defeat the
point. The fix isn't a setting. It's a different kind of ownership, one where the people who use
the thing are the people who own it, so there's no outside buyer your attention is being grown for.

---

## The written version (a short paragraph, ends on the pointer)

For twenty years the case against big social platforms has been about privacy: they can see you.
It hasn't moved anyone, because the harm is invisible and the convenience is right there. But
exposure was never the real problem. The real problem is authorship. An advertising-funded platform
doesn't merely carry your relationships, it shapes their content and texture, the rhythm of who you
hear from, the slow drift of what feels normal, all tuned toward an objective you didn't choose:
your attention, packaged and sold. You are not the customer in that arrangement. Your relationships
are the inventory. And by the platform's own internal admission, the things that hook you are often
the things you say you don't even like, which is why the shaping has to stay quiet, because a feed
that announced it was optimizing you against your own preferences would stop working. None of this
is fate. It's a property of a particular *form*: who funds and owns the thing. Change the form so
that the people who use it are the people who own it, with no outside buyer in the field, and your
attention stops being a crop. That form has a name, a cooperative, and the longer argument is that
it's the only form that can actually host relationships among equals, and that it has to be built
that way from the start, because you can't convert into it once the harvest machinery is installed.

# Adoption strategy: crossing the chasm on purpose

status: strategy-in-progress (open thread T11). register: this is the go-to-market side of the
adoption question, the argument for what a new entrant *does* about the adoption chasm. It is a
direction under active development, not a committed plan. The descriptive field fact this responds
to (why incumbents win, why almost nothing crosses from a centered platform to an alternative, the
observation that only Signal has reached mainstream non-technical users) is mapped in the fenced
layer at `../fenced/platform-dominance-and-adoption.md` and is not reproduced here. This doc carries
only the strategic response.

## Overview

A challenger entering an entrenched field does not get to skip the chasm; it has to cross it on
purpose. The descriptive map says the crossing is rare and that organic pull alone does not produce
it. The strategic reading of that map is the useful part: if crossing does not happen by hoping for
migration, then it happens by *assembling the conditions deliberately*, and by picking entry moves
(the bridges) that let a small entrant borrow scale it has not yet earned.

Three moves organize the strategy. First, hold the chasm thesis honestly, so the plan is built for
the crossing that actually occurs rather than the one we would prefer. Second, choose among the
bridges (network-effect seeding, institutional mandate, a wedge use-case) rather than betting the
whole entry on one. Third, run the enablement tactics that make whichever bridge we pick actually
carry weight. None of this is settled; it is the shape of the argument as it currently stands.

## The chasm thesis (the strategic premise)

The *chasm* framing is Geoffrey Moore's (*Crossing the Chasm*, 1991) [CITE]: in the technology
adoption lifecycle, the hard gap sits between the early adopters, who buy a vision, and the early
majority, who buy a working product that already fits their life — and most challengers fall into it.
We borrow the frame and apply it to this field; the descriptive account of why decentralized and
local-first challengers in particular fail to cross lives in the fenced layer (source-of-record there,
not re-argued here).

The premise we build on: among the surveyed decentralized and local-first projects, essentially one
crossed to mainstream non-technical users, and it did so by making its hard technology invisible and
matching the incumbent experience rather than asking users to care about the architecture. The full
descriptive account of why the field behaves this way lives in the fenced layer; the point that
matters *for strategy* is narrower.

Crossing appears to require three things present at the same time, not any one alone:

- **Product parity.** The alternative has to be at least as good to use as the incumbent for the
  thing the user actually came to do. A decentralization story does not substitute for parity;
  users do not adopt an argument, they adopt an experience.

- **A non-extractive sustaining org.** Something has to keep the lights on without monetizing the
  users in the way the incumbents do, because the whole reason to switch collapses if the
  alternative reproduces the extraction. Donation-and-grant funding has repeatedly proven fragile
  here; a durable, member-aligned model is part of the product, not a back-office detail.

- **An inciting event.** A trigger (a policy change at an incumbent, a surveillance scandal, a
  connectivity shutdown) is what actually moves people. The hard lesson is that inciting events
  produce *spikes, not sustained migration*. A spike is an opportunity to convert, not a win in
  itself; if parity and the sustaining org are not already in place when the spike arrives, the
  attention drains back out.

The strategic consequence: parity and the sustaining org are the standing investments we control,
and the inciting event is the moment we have to be *ready* for rather than the thing we can plan to
cause. Build so that when a spike comes, it lands on a product people will stay with.

## The bridges a new entrant can use

A bridge is an entry move that lets a small challenger borrow reach it cannot generate on its own.
Three are on the table, and they are not mutually exclusive; the likely play is to lead with one and
keep the others open.

- **Network-effect seeding (embed in existing trust).** The recurring failure mode of the field is
  expecting a trust network to *form around* a new tool. The stronger move is the inverse: enter a
  trust network that already exists (a family, a neighborhood, a professional community, a club) and
  become the tool it uses, so the graph arrives with the first users instead of having to be built
  from zero. This directly answers the cold-start / empty-room problem: a seeded group is populated
  on day one because its members already know each other. The corollary is that the earliest target
  is a *whole small group*, not a scatter of individuals.

- **Institutional mandate (the fourth bridge).** The one project that reached institutional scale
  did it top-down: institutions adopted it and brought their members along, rather than individuals
  choosing it one at a time. That is a distinct bridge from consumer pull, and it is worth designing
  for explicitly rather than treating as an accident. The entry points are institutions whose values
  already align with a member-owned, non-extractive posture (cooperatives, unions, NGOs, mission
  organizations); when such a body adopts the tool, its membership is the seeded network. Designing
  the product and the onboarding so an institution *can* mandate it (administration, provisioning,
  the sustaining-org story an institution can trust) is currently an undone directive, not a shipped
  capability.

- **A wedge use-case.** Rather than competing head-on for the incumbent's whole surface, enter
  through a narrow case where the peer-to-peer approach is the *only* option or the obviously better
  one (off-grid or low-connectivity contexts, or a specific activity the incumbents serve poorly).
  The wedge gives real users with a real reason to be there before parity across the full surface is
  achieved. An owned starting pond (a bounded community or activity we run) is the candidate wedge;
  it is a *candidate*, not a validated answer, and finding the wedge that both has genuine pull and
  bridges outward to the general case is itself open work.

## Enablement tactics

Tactics are the concrete work that makes a bridge carry weight. They accrete as the case builds;
today they are directions, not a backlog.

- **Close the parity gaps deliberately.** Name the specific experience gaps against the incumbent
  for the wedge use-case and treat closing them as adoption work, not polish. Parity is the price of
  entry, not a nice-to-have.

- **Make the sustaining-org story legible.** The non-extractive model has to be something a
  prospective user or a mandating institution can understand and trust, not just a value we hold
  internally. Its clarity is part of what makes the switch credible.

- **Be ready for the spike.** Since inciting events cannot be manufactured, the tactic is
  readiness: onboarding that survives a sudden influx, a retention path that converts spike traffic
  into embedded groups, and instrumentation to learn from a spike when it comes.

- **Answer cold-start with seeded groups.** The empty-room problem is solved by arriving with a
  group, not a person. Lean on the network-effect-seeding bridge as the default first-user story,
  and keep accreting better cold-start answers than the current candidate wedge.

## What this establishes (and does not)

Establishes the current shape of the go-to-market argument: the chasm is crossed by assembling
parity, a non-extractive sustaining org, and readiness for an inciting event, and by leading with
one of three bridges (network-effect seeding, institutional mandate, a wedge use-case) to borrow
reach a small entrant cannot generate alone. It frames the strategic response to the field's
adoption dynamics without reproducing the descriptive map of why incumbents win, which lives in the
fenced layer.

Does not settle a plan. This is open thread T11: the bridges are not yet chosen or sequenced, the
wedge use-case is a candidate rather than a validated entry point, the institutional-mandate posture
is an undone design directive, and the sustaining-org model has its own gates to clear. Treat this
as the direction the adoption argument is trending, to be firmed up as the case accretes evidence
and the org model is settled.

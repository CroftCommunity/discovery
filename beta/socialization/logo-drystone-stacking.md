## The concept

Drystone stacking (mortarless stone building) as the mark. A small collection of representative dry-stacked stones stands in for the technique itself: no mortar, no keystone, just balanced irregular stones.

This ties directly to the drystone and cairn naming family. A cairn is a set of dry-stacked waymarker stones, built without mortar and without a keystone, which makes the stacking motif the literal picture of the name.

## The direction

- **Central motif:** a sturdy stack of irregular dry-stacked stones as the focal point, the stacking technique itself carrying the meaning.

- **Natural context:** the stones set in a grounded rural landscape (rolling hill, a solitary tree, a winding path, distant field) for a recognizable, earthy setting.

- **Palette:** natural and earthy tones, kept few and distinct.

- **Badge treatment:** a circular emblem with rugged sans-serif border type, a traditional patch layout that reads both on screen and in physical form.

## The embroidery constraint

This is the hard constraint that drives every other decision: the mark must be a simple, embroidery-able form. That means few colors, stitch geometry that simplifies cleanly, and legibility at small size on a patch, hat, or uniform.

The mark must survive as threadwork and stay distinct when reduced. Practically, the deliverable is a vector form plus a one-color reduction and a two-color reduction, so the stones and landscape hold together whether rendered crisply on screen or stitched at small scale.

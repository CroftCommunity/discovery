# Tilling the Soil of Our Relationships

### On what social platforms actually do, and the choice we still have

---

There is a way of seeing the large social platforms that, once you see it, is hard to
un-see. They are farmers. We are not the customers in their fields. We are the ground.

This is not an insult thrown at them. It is a description of how the thing is built, and
the description happens to be precise. Walk it out slowly, because the precision is what
makes it matter.

---

## The farmer and the field

A farmer does not love the soil for its own sake. The soil is a means. It is tilled,
turned, fed, and worked toward one thing: the yield. The farmer attends to the soil's
condition carefully, but only, and exactly, insofar as that condition bears on the
harvest. Healthy soil is good news to a farmer the way a full warehouse is good news to
a shipper. It is good *because of what it produces*.

Now look at a social platform whose money comes from advertising. The soil is our
relationships, the web of human connection between us and the people we know. The crop
is our attention. The farmer is the platform. And the buyer waiting at the market is the
advertiser.

The platform tends the soil. It really does. It builds features that help us find each
other, that surface the people we have lost touch with, that make a moment of connection
easy. This is genuine, in the way a farmer genuinely cares for the field. But the caring
points somewhere. It is cultivation toward a harvest. The harvest is attention, and
the attention is sold. The connection is the means. The yield is the point.

That is why the warm language, *bringing the world closer together*, *meaningful social
interaction*, *community*, is not exactly a lie. It is the farmer's account of the soil.
The soil's health matters to the story. It is simply not the reason the field exists.

---

## What monocropping does to ground

Here is where the picture turns from uncomfortable to important.

A farmer who plants one crop, year after year, for one buyer, changes the soil. Growing
the single thing that sells selects *for* whatever feeds that crop and *against* everything
else. The ground is not just harvested. It is reshaped in the image of the harvest.

Our relationships are reshaped the same way. When the crop is attention, the field gets
worked toward whatever produces attention: the heated argument, the outrage, the post you
cannot help clicking, the pull that brings you back at midnight. And it gets worked *away
from* whatever does not produce attention: the quiet exchange, the conversation that
reaches a resolution and ends, the connection that is simply enough and asks for nothing
more. Those are the healthy parts of a relationship. They are also, to an attention farmer,
fallow ground. They do not yield. So they are not cultivated, and slowly they are crowded
out.

This is not a worry someone made up. When researchers have been able to measure it
carefully, they have found that the systems ranking our feeds amplify emotionally charged,
hostile content, and that they do it *beyond* the choices we made about who to follow, and
even beyond what we ourselves say we want to see. In one careful study of a major
platform's feed, people did not even prefer the heated political posts the algorithm
served them. The field is being worked toward the crop the farmer can sell, not the crop
we would have grown. The ground is left changed in the shape of the harvest.

---

## The thing the metaphor gets right by getting it wrong

Press on the image and it breaks in one specific place. The break is the whole point,
so do not skip past it.

Soil has no say. That is what makes it soil. It has no perspective that counts, no life of
its own that registers as a claim. In the farmer's world, the ground exists to be worked,
and its only relevant feature is what it grows. The entire frame belongs to the cultivator.

But our relationships are not soil. They are *us*. They are made of people, and people are
not ground. A person has standing. A person's view of their own life is not an input to
someone else's calculation. It is the thing that matters in its own right. To call our
relationships "soil to be tilled" is already to have taken the farmer's seat, the one place
from which people *can* be treated as mere substrate for a yield.

So the metaphor is exactly true as a picture of *how the platform must see us*, and exactly
false as a picture of *what we are*. That gap, between how the form sees us and what we
actually are, is not a flaw in the comparison. It is the indictment, made plain. The trouble
with an attention-farming platform is not that it tends the field badly. It is that it can
*only* relate to the field the way a farmer relates to ground, and we are not ground.

It cannot do otherwise, and this is the part worth sitting with. The platform's purpose has
a place in it for yield and for the conditions of yield. It has no place in it for the
question of whether your relationships have a life of their own worth caring about on their
own terms. That question does not get a low score. It does not get scored. It is not small
in the platform's eyes. It is invisible, because there is nothing in the machine that could
see it.

---

## Why this is worth thinking through at all

You might ask: why bother walking out something this carefully? Here is why.

Because the question underneath is not technical and not abstract. It is this:

*Do you want the content of your human relationships, not just the apps you use to reach
people, but the actual texture of how you connect with them, tilled, turned, and reshaped
for someone else's harvest?*

Not the medium. The relationships themselves. The shape of your conversations, the rhythm of
who you hear from and when, the slow drift of what feels normal to say and feel. Those are
being cultivated, right now, toward a crop you do not own and a buyer you will never meet.

And the agent doing the cultivating is not evil. It is something stranger and harder to argue
with. It is *amoral with a narrow purpose*. It bears your relationships no malice. It is
simply indifferent to them as anything other than feedstock, and indifference with a purpose
is enough to reshape a field. There is no villain to appeal to, because there is no one home
in the way there would need to be for an appeal to land. There is only the form, doing what
the form does.

---

## The choice

Here is the part that is not despair.

None of this is fate. It is a property of a particular *form*, a particular way of funding
and owning a platform, in which the people are the ground and the advertiser is the customer.
Change the form and you change what is possible.

There is a form in which the people who use the thing and the people who own the thing are the
same people. Where there is no outside buyer in the field, because the field and the farmer
are one body. In that form your attention is not inventory, because there is no third party it
is being grown for. Your relationships are not a crop, because no one outside them is waiting
to harvest. The plainest name for that form is a *cooperative*, owned by its members, governed
by them, accountable to them and to no external claim.

That accountability is not a hope you bring to it. It is something the form lets you write into
the charter and bind, the way you cannot bind it inside a company that answers to tradable
shares. The form does not make a platform good on its own. A cooperative is only as aligned as
the people who charter and govern it make it. What the form does is make the alignment
*possible to hold to*, which is the one thing the advertising-funded form cannot offer at all.

That is not a utopian wish. It is a structural choice, and it is available. The argument for
why this is the *only* form that can host genuine peer relationships, and why it has to be built
that way from the very beginning, because you cannot convert into it once the harvest machinery
is installed, is the longer companion to this piece. But the heart of it fits in a sentence.

They can only farm us. They can only till, uproot, and disturb the soil of our relationships
for attention, because that is their entire purpose.

We are not soil to be tilled.

Or, and this is the whole of it, we can choose not to be.

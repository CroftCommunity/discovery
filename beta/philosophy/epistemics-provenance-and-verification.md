# Epistemics: provenance and verification

register: philosophy layer, focused note. status: AI-surfaced argument, fact-checking pending against primary sources (marked inline).

**What this note is.** The epistemic case for a provenance-first substrate: how truth-claims actually get verified, why closed-loop institutional authority let flawed and possibly fabricated data survive for decades, and why the falling cost of verification is the structural shift the design leans on. It sits beside the peer-standing argument as a second pure-philosophy line: peer-standing establishes what a center *is not entitled to represent*; this establishes what a center *cannot reliably certify*, namely truth.

## The case study: Rosenhan and the appetite that carried it

Rosenhan's "On Being Sane in Insane Places" (Science, 1973) reported that eight healthy pseudopatients presented at twelve psychiatric hospitals across five states claiming to hear voices saying "empty," "hollow," "thud" [UNVERIFIED: confirm exact wording against the primary paper]; once admitted they behaved normally, were never detected as sane, and were released as "schizophrenia in remission." Ordinary note-taking was recorded by staff as pathology (logged as "engages in writing behavior" [UNVERIFIED]). By the study's own account, a share of the actual patients voiced suspicion the pseudopatients were not genuinely ill.

The study did not survive on its methodology. It survived on the intellectual appetite of its moment. The anti-psychiatry movement of the 1960s and 70s supplied the cultural fuel: Thomas Szasz, in The Myth of Mental Illness (1961), argued that mental illness is a social and legal construct enforcing conformity; R. D. Laing pressed adjacent claims. The study "proved" the establishment was incompetent or oppressive, and because that conclusion matched the appetite of the time, its methodological weaknesses were waved through. The data and the sentiment were codependent: each borrowed authority from the other.

The later critique is itself CONTESTED. Susannah Cahalan, in The Great Pretender (2019), argued that Rosenhan likely fabricated or distorted data, on the grounds that surviving pseudopatient accounts conflicted with hospital records. This is a serious challenge to the 1973 narrative's factual integrity, and it is a challenge, not a settled verdict. The relevant epistemic point does not depend on how it resolves.

## The load-bearing point: verification latency collapsed

The point is about how long it takes, and how much it costs, to check a claim.

In 1973, challenging a study like this was close to impossible for an ordinary reader. It required the unpublished files, access to anonymous subjects, and cross-state record audits. A reader could not sanity-check the data at all. Authority therefore rested on the prestige of the journal and the researcher: a trust-the-expert regime, where the credential stood in for the evidence because the evidence was unreachable.

Today the cost of verification is near zero. A significant paper enters a decentralized, effectively global critique layer; thin data or suspect methods surface on public forums such as PubPeer in days or weeks, not forty years. The regime shifted from "trust the expert" toward a system designed to stress-test claims in something close to real time.

A side effect is worth naming plainly: truth *feels* more fragile now, because the demolition happens in public and in view. That feeling is misleading. Fabrication did not necessarily increase. Detection caught up to the frequency of publication.

The replication crisis is the institutional version of the same event. It is an acknowledgment that the way science was conducted and vetted for decades was structurally incapable of guaranteeing that its results were true. Pre-internet, the gatekeepers (journals, editors, tenure committees) were the only filters, and the crisis could not fully manifest because decentralized real-time auditing did not exist. Post-internet, once raw datasets were shared digitally, the gap between the published narrative and the underlying evidence became visible to anyone with a browser. The crisis was not a decline in scientific integrity; it was the verification infrastructure finally catching up to the claims already on the books.

## Two heuristics, set by the formative environment

Which heuristic a person defaults to tracks less with age than with the cognitive environment of their formative years.

Those formed when information was scarce and expensive default to an *authority-based* heuristic: if it is in print, or comes from an institution, presume it true. In an environment where verification was genuinely unreachable, this was a rational adaptation, not a failing.

Those formed when information is abundant and cheap need an *adversarial* heuristic: if something is presented as true, interrogate it and cross-reference it. Thinking adversarially without sliding into cynicism is a manual override of the consensus-trusting brain. It is learned rather than inherited, and it takes effort to hold.

## The tail-risk: bubbles that fit the priors

The adversarial heuristic has a failure mode, and it is the one this design must respect rather than wish away.

The scarcity problem was "not enough data." The abundance problem is "too much data that fits our priors." Having the tools to verify is only half of it. The other half is the discipline not to turn those tools into instruments for hardening beliefs already held, selecting only the confirmations and auditing only the challenges.

Adversarial verification is powerful, and against tribal or ideological priors it is insufficient on its own. A verification layer that people use only to confirm what their group already believes does not produce truth; it produces better-defended bubbles.

## The design bridge: provenance-first, locally verifiable, content-addressed

This is the epistemic case under two commitments the design already carries: "no center can hold the truth," and "compute provenance, never utility."

Closed-loop, centralized, high-latency verification is precisely what let flawed and possibly fabricated data survive. The structural answer is to invert where authority lives. In a provenance-first, locally verifiable, content-addressed substrate, a claim carries its own auditable chain rather than resting on a center's prestige. Verification does not require reaching the center and trusting it; the chain travels with the claim, and anyone can check it locally. That is the same collapse of verification latency, made a property of the substrate instead of an accident of the era.

The tail-risk sets the limit. Provenance answers "can I check where this came from." It does not answer "will I check honestly, or only to confirm my side." So provenance must be paired with real-humanity grounding. Adversarial verification alone loses to tribal priors, and a substrate that ships only cryptographic auditability, without the human grounding that makes people willing to be wrong, will produce well-provenanced bubbles rather than shared truth.

## What this establishes (and does not)

Establishes that centralized, high-latency verification is a structural cause (not merely an unlucky one) of flawed and fabricated claims surviving, that the falling cost of verification is the real content of the replication crisis, and that a provenance-first, locally verifiable, content-addressed substrate is the structural answer because it lets a claim carry its own auditable chain rather than rest on a center's prestige.

Does **not** claim that provenance produces truth on its own, resolve the CONTESTED Rosenhan-fabrication question, or verify the quoted details against the primary sources (those remain flagged). It also does not specify a mechanism; it states the requirement that any mechanism must pair auditable provenance with real-humanity grounding, because adversarial verification alone loses to tribal priors.

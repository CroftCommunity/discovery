# Crofting, dry-stone, and the enclosure inversion

status: material-history record (Layer 1). Verification flags are load-bearing; unverified claims stay flagged, and refuted claims are not carried.

The project takes its form and its names from a real thing and a real space: a smallholding coupled to a surviving common, walls stacked without mortar, and cairns raised by many hands to mark a path. This doc records that material and cultural lineage: what a croft actually is (stripped of romance), how dry-stone construction and the cairn work, the long enclosure of the commons and the croft as the rare place the pendulum stopped, the etymology of the three names (croft, drystone, cairn), the enclosure poets who left the interior witness, and the arc from the enclosure of land to the enclosure of connection itself. It is the "why it resonates" record. The principled argument for why the design is right lives in `philosophy/`; the present-tense harm case against incumbent platforms lives in `activism/`. This doc points to those and does not reproduce them.

## 1. The word is a concept, not a definition

The Scottish crofting tenure most people associate with the word (codified by the Crofters' Holdings (Scotland) Act 1886) is a nineteenth-century formalization. The word predates it by roughly eight centuries. "Croft" is Old English, a small enclosed field often beside a dwelling, attested early enough that it fossilized across English toponymy long before any statute. Most of the dated record is not prose at all but place-names (Stancroft 1175, Oxecroft 1288, Barleycroft 1423) and locative surnames (Hugo de Croft, 1163). That is the fingerprint of a word that was common property of the language, spread by ordinary land-naming, not handed down by an authority.

The Middle English Dictionary's own sense-split (entry `croft n.`, MED10396, open-access and directly checkable) is the tell. Within roughly the same span, *croft* names a working plot of oats, a tournament arena, an enclosure in the abstract, and, in the morality plays, hell itself ("Com to my crofte [hell]", *Towneley Plays*). A word that can mean a field, an arena, and hell is a spatial concept (enclosed ground put to use), not a fixed referent. The etymology beyond Old English is genuinely uncertain, with no clean cognate chain; the cognates that do exist (Dutch *krocht*, a field on the downs; Low German *kroch*, enclosed farmland) point to shared West Germanic inheritance, not export. No single-source etymology ever stuck because the concept was already distributed across the western Germanic world.

The verb confirms it. "Crofting" as an active verb is first attested in 1772, in the Manchester Directory, in the Lancashire cloth-bleaching trade, not Highland farming: a "crofter" or "whitster" laid linen out to whiten on an enclosed grass field also called a croft. Bleaching cloth and growing oats were both just "what you do on a croft," the same concept specialized by industry. (The 1772 date is solid; the exact verbatim sentence and the bleaching-versus-farming sense are [UNVERIFIED], behind the OED paywall.)

## 2. The croft: the smallholding, the trap, and the balance

A croft is, in the old joke, "a small piece of land entirely surrounded by legislation." Not a house: a small piece of agricultural land (a micro-farm) with a strict legal definition; the cottage is incidental, the land and the tenancy are the croft. Historically crofts were tenancies (rent to a landlord, protection from eviction while the land is worked and the rent paid, and the tenancy heritable). A township is a cluster of these, with individual arable "inbye" plus shared "common grazing": a dual structure, individual enterprise coupled to communal working.

The de-romanticized history matters more than the heroic myth, and it cuts against the popular telling. The communal, hereditary relationship (the Gaelic *dùthchas*, the people's hereditary stake in clan territory as common property) predates crofting; but crofting itself was largely invented by the same landlords who did the clearing. The new crofts were deliberately sized too small to live on: a contemporary estimate held that a crofter needed some 200 days of work away from the croft to avoid destitution. That was engineered, not incidental. A half-starved tenant is a captive labour force, and the deficit was filled by selling labour back to the same party who controlled the land (kelp, fishing). Crofting in origin was less a peasant idyll than a labour-tying arrangement dressed as land reform.

This is not a revisionist flourish but the settled reading of the modern Clearances scholarship. T. M. Devine (*The Scottish Clearances*, 2018; *Clanship to Crofters' War*), James Hunter (*The Making of the Crofting Community*, 1976), and Eric Richards (*Debating the Highland Clearances*) are the authorities behind the strip-the-romance account carried here: the landlords eliminating the tacksmen to capture their cut, the new crofts engineered undersized, and the whole arrangement read from the documentary record rather than the heroic folk telling. Where §2 says the de-romanticized history cuts against the popular version, this is whose history it is.

The honest limit on this reading is the genocide framing, which the same scholarship treats as genuinely contested. Devine holds it "ludicrous to suggest a genocide" and finds "no direct evidence of increasing mortality caused by clearances" (the Highland population actually rose across 1801 to the 1850s); others hold that "ethnic cleansing" fits the deliberate removal. It is a live argument among serious people, and this doc carries the Clearances as engineered dispossession without asserting the stronger charge. That is the trap the name carries honestly, and it is why it reads across to software: just enough space to be present, never enough to be independent, the deficit filled by working for the landlord. Naming the project after the croft names the trap first, not the triumph.

## 3. Enclosure, and the croft as the settlement point where the pendulum stopped

"Enclosure" sits at the centre of two opposite stories. The historical Enclosure *movement* dissolved the open commons into consolidated private holdings and extinguished common right, pushing the "surplus" people off the land. The crofting *system* did the near-opposite: it locked in a secure individual plot and kept the township's common grazing.

```
HISTORICAL ENCLOSURE               THE CROFT (the rare settlement)
  open commons + shared waste        secure individual plot ┐
       │                                                     ├ coupled by design
       ▼                             retained share in the  ┘
  consolidated private holdings;     township's COMMON GRAZING
  common right EXTINGUISHED          → the common SURVIVES beside the private plot
```

The croft is not a pristine survival from before enclosure. The honest sequence is communal *runrig* townships, then the Clearances breaking them for sheep (Highland enclosure in all but name), then the croft as a negotiated re-balancing that locked in a private plot plus a surviving common. It is the settlement point where the pendulum stopped, the compromise England never reached.

The win was real but partial. The Crofters' War of the 1880s (rent strikes and land-raids onto the common grazings at the Battle of the Braes, 1882, and Glendale, 1883) produced not a scaffold but the Napier Commission and the 1886 Act. The Napier evidence preserved the verbatim testimony of hundreds of crofters, most speaking in Gaelic; the first witness, Angus Stewart of Braes (8 May 1883), summarised the remedy as "give us land." The Sheriff of Inverness-shire, William Ivory, reported that the Braes crowd, many women and children among them, "attacked us furiously first with curses, and then with sticks, stones and other missiles" (NRS, GD1/36/1/3/52). The Act secured tenancy, fair rents, heritability, and protection of the common grazing, and it pointedly refused to return the cleared land. It is remembered grandly as "the Magna Carta of the Highlands and Islands" (attribution to the historian John Lorne Campbell is [UNVERIFIED]; two phrasings circulate), and the grandeur is apt precisely as the original Magna Carta was: a real limit on arbitrary power that fell well short of liberation. The truest modern echo was community ownership (Assynt, 1993; Eigg, 1997), not the hedged individual right-to-buy: the meaningful unit is the community holding the commons together, not the atomised private deed.

The colour of the Clearances comes mostly from the popular re-telling and stays flagged. The animals that displaced the Gaels were bitterly called "the lairds' four-footed clansmen" [UNVERIFIED]; Robert Chambers is quoted approving the substitution of sheep for "innumerable hordes of useless human beings" [UNVERIFIED]; a Shetland memory records an evicted elder laying a curse on the laird as the smoke of the burning homes darkened the sky [UNVERIFIED]. The friction still surfaces (a modern land-court case is cited in which the crofter prevailed over a landlord's tree-planting scheme on common grazing) [UNVERIFIED].

The English record is overwhelmingly resistance in defeat. John Ball's couplet from the 1381 Peasants' Revolt carries the bedrock idea that hierarchy over land is made, not natural:

> When Adam delved and Eve span, / Who was then the gentleman?

Ball was hanged, drawn, and quartered. Kett tore down the hedges (1549) and was hanged from Norwich Castle. The 1607 Newton rising coined the two names for the whole tradition: the crowds "levelled" the hedges and "dug" up the ditches and so were called Levellers or Diggers. In 1649 Gerrard Winstanley's True Levellers began cultivating the common at St George's Hill and declared the aim to make "the Earth a common treasury for all, both rich and poor" (primary text); the colony was broken up within a year. The anonymous protest verse names the asymmetry exactly:

> The law locks up the man or woman / Who steals the goose from off the common, / But lets the greater felon loose / Who steals the common from the goose.

The verse's dating is [UNVERIFIED]: it is often cited mid-to-late eighteenth century, with hard attestations at 1816 and 1821.

The pattern is global, which is why the croft matters rather than being a local curiosity. The same move recurs wherever a society intensifies agriculture, markets, or state power: Rome's *ager publicus* and the Gracchi (133 BC), Bengal's Permanent Settlement (1793), Stolypin's breaking of the Russian commune (1906), Mexico's *ejido* (dissolved 1856, restored 1917, re-privatized 1992, triggering the Zapatista rising), and the American West (the Homestead Act 1862, barbed wire as the literal fence, and the Dawes Act 1887 carving the Native commons into allotments, the closest analogue to the Highland clearances). The justification is always identical: communal tenure cast as backward, private parceling as "Improvement." Mexico's pendulum shows the commons can return and be taken again; the fight is never finally settled.

The intellectual anchor under this reading is worth naming, because the doc otherwise argues enclosure-as-theft at length without the authorities the argument rests on. Karl Marx gave the process its lasting name in *Capital* vol. 1 (1867): *so-called primitive accumulation*, the forcible expropriation of the people from the soil that manufactures a class with nothing to sell but its labour. That is the canonical scholarly name (the title of Part VIII, chs. 26–33) for the exact move this section traces, and in the chapter on the expropriation of the common lands (ch. 27) Marx named the enclosure of communal property as bluntly as its mechanism:

> the systematic theft of communal property

[Verbatim in the Ben Fowkes / Penguin (1976) translation of *Capital* vol. 1, ch. 27; the older Moore-Aveling translation renders the same passage "the systematic robbery of the communal lands," so the exact words are translation-dependent — confirm against the edition cited before publish. The phrase is Marx's about the enclosure of common lands specifically, not a summary label for primitive accumulation as a whole.] And the "Improvement" justification — communal tenure recast as inefficiency — has its own direct rebuttal. Garrett Hardin's *The Tragedy of the Commons* (1968) is the canonical modern statement that a shared resource is doomed to overuse; Elinor Ostrom's *Governing the Commons* (1990) refuted it empirically, documenting real common-pool resources (the Törbel alpine commons, stably managed for centuries) governed sustainably by their own users without either privatization or a state. On the specific historical case the correction is sharper still: historians read Hardin as bad history, because the English commons were not overused but stolen — enclosed, not exhausted. That is the difference between calling enclosure an efficiency and calling it a theft, and it is why the "communal tenure is inefficient" rhetoric named just above is an argument beta can answer rather than merely assert. (Ostrom's governed-commons framework itself is developed in `philosophy/` and `drystone-spec/`; here it is carried only in the enclosure-rebuttal register.)

The honest disanalogy stays visible: land is rivalrous and finite, virtual space is closer to non-rivalrous and reproducible (you can fork, you can exit; the lock-in is network effects and data gravity, not physical scarcity). The parts of the croft pattern the project claims are the engineered dependency it means to invert, the private-plot-plus-living-common as the architecture, and the maintenance burden as permanent. Where the land analogy stops carrying weight, it is said so.

## 4. John Clare and the enclosure poets

The interior, emotional record belongs to John Clare (1793 to 1864), an agricultural labourer from Helpston who watched his own parish enclosed (award 1820). His texts are public domain; the exact spelling and punctuation are editor-dependent, since Clare left those to his editors.

*The Mores* stages enclosure as conquest and imprisonment:

> Inclosure came and trampled on the grave / Of labour's rights and left the poor a slave

and it marks the fenced-out paths and the closed ways of childhood:

> Each little tyrant with his little sign / Shows where man claims earth glows no more divine

> paths to freedom and to childhood dear / A board sticks up to notice 'no road here'

*Remembrances* mourns particular named places lost, grieving specific trees and nooks the way one grieves individuals, and it stages enclosure as a conqueror levelling the whole living field:

> Inclosure like a Buonaparte let not a thing remain / It levelled every bush and tree and levelled every hill / And hung the moles for traitors – though the brook is running still

[UNVERIFIED, confirm against primary edition before publish]

*To a Fallen Elm* gives the tradition its tightest line, turning the enclosers' own word back on them:

> With axe at root he felled thee to the ground / And barked of freedom, O I hate that sound / It grows the cant terms of enslaving tools / To wrong another by the name of right.

and it sharpens the same turn to a couplet, naming self-interest as the tyrant that fells whatever stands in freedom's way:

> Self interest saw thee stand in freedoms ways / So thy old shadow must a tyrant be

[UNVERIFIED, confirm against primary edition before publish]

"Improvement" and "freedom" as the enclosers' cant is the exact rhetoric of the global pattern above, and it rhymes forward to the platform's "free" tier.

## 5. Dry-stone and the cairn: building without mortar

Two of the project's names come straight from mortarless stone construction, and they are material facts before they are metaphors.

A dry-stone wall is built without mortar. It holds because of the variation in its stones, not in spite of it: huge footing boulders, full-width through-stones that tie the two faces together, and small hearting stones packed in the core do different work but are equal in importance, and there is no keystone. That is the material lineage behind the name **Drystone** (the peer-to-peer protocol). What the wall means for the protocol (peers equal in rights while their capabilities differ, and why a network that pretends all peers are identical lets the strongest quietly become an authority) is argued in `philosophy/`; here the record is only that the name is taken from a real way of building that holds without a single load-bearing keystone.

A **cairn** is dry-stacked waymarker stones, raised by many hands, with no mortar and no keystone, left by those who went before to mark a path for those who come after. It shares the dry-stone construction family with the wall: the cairn catalogues the stones, the wall is built from them. The name was carried over to mark the index of the surrounding field of building blocks.

The naming discipline is worth recording plainly: these names are taken from real construction and real land, and some proposed name-stories have been refuted and are not carried. The names kept are the ones that survive as material fact (croft as a use-shape older than the statute; the dry-stone wall and the cairn as they are actually built).

## 6. The arc to the present: connection itself as the newest enclosure

The enclosure did not stop at land, or at the informational commons that followed. Its newest target is connection itself. Having enclosed the physical commons and then the informational one, the growth machine moved inward to enclose human relationship: the death of the third place, manufactured loneliness, and platforms that rent our relationships back to us (pay-to-boost visibility, feeds tuned for outrage and comparison, a charge levied on simply being heard by the people who already chose you). Making communication and connection a product sold back at a premium is the saddest turn of the cycle, and it is the turn that explains why the answer has to be a *social* one: a protocol for documents or files would leave the enclosed commons enclosed, and only a commons for relationship contests the enclosure where it now actually lives.

That connection is being enclosed is the historical arc this doc carries. *Why* that enclosure is wrong (the principled case, relational equality, non-domination) is argued in `philosophy/`, and the present-tense indictment of the platforms doing it lives in `activism/`. This section names the pattern and hands off.

## What this establishes (and does not)

Establishes the material and cultural precedent the form rests on: that "croft" names a use-shape older and wider than the Scottish statute (so a substrate is named accurately); that the croft is engineered dependency (the trap) and a private plot coupled to a surviving common (the balance); that the enclosure move is global and recurring and the croft is the rare documented halt; that dry-stone construction and the cairn are real, mortarless, keystone-less ways of building that the names are taken from; that the enclosure poets left the interior witness; and that the arc runs from the enclosure of land to the enclosure of connection itself.

Does not make the principled argument for why the design is right (that is `philosophy/`), argue the present harm case against incumbent platforms (that is `activism/`), or carry the human-facing pitch. It also does not claim the land analogy ports cleanly (virtual space is non-rivalrous and exit is cheap), and it keeps unverified colour flagged rather than presenting folklore as settled fact.
